Rouché's theorem: where the textbook proof has a gap, and the simplest repair

Re: CA notes, Theorem 3.6.3. Setup: $f,g$ holomorphic on $U$, $\gamma$ a simple contour with its interior in $U$, and $|f|>|g|$ on $\gamma^\ast$. Put $$h=\frac{f+g}{f}=1+\frac{g}{f}.$$ Write $\operatorname{ord}_a(\cdot)$ for the vanishing order at a point $a$ (positive $=$ zero, negative $=$ pole, $0$ $=$ neither), and let $N_f,\,N_{f+g}$ be the number of zeros inside $\gamma$, counted with multiplicity.

The sentence is true — but only one way

The notes say: "its zeros are zeros of $f+g$ and its poles are zeros of $f$." As implications these are correct:

$$a \text{ a pole of } h \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(h)<0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f)>\operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)\ge 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; a \text{ a zero of } f,$$ $$a \text{ a zero of } h \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(h)>0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)>\operatorname{ord}_a(f)\ge 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; a \text{ a zero of } f+g.$$

The trouble is the converse. A zero of $f+g$ need not be a zero of $h$: if $f$ also vanishes there to equal-or-higher order, the quotient cancels and $\operatorname{ord}_a(h)\le 0$. So the map "zeros/poles of $h$" $\leftrightarrow$ "zeros of $f+g$ / zeros of $f$" is not a multiplicity-preserving bijection, and the proof's jump from

$$Z_h = P_h \qquad\text{(the winding-number argument)}$$

to "$\,f$ and $f+g$ have the same number of zeros$\,$" is not justified by the quoted sentence.

Sharpest witness: $g=f/2$. Then $|g|=\tfrac12|f|<|f|$ (hypothesis holds), but $h=\tfrac{3}{2}$ is constant: no zeros, no poles. Yet every zero of $f$ is an equal-order zero of $f+g=\tfrac32 f$. So here every zero of $f+g$ is one of the "unaccounted" coincident zeros — none of them is a zero of $h$.

The repair: stay in the holomorphic argument principle, connect by the log-derivative

The fix is not to count "net order" of $h$ — that quietly upgrades the argument principle to a meromorphic, net-order form the notes never prove, which is the very thing in dispute. Instead, apply the argument principle only to holomorphic functions with no zeros on $\gamma^\ast$, where it is exactly the unambiguous statement the notes give:

For $F$ holomorphic on $U$ with $F\neq 0$ on $\gamma^\ast$, $$\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{F'(z)}{F(z)}\,dz \;=\; N_F \quad(\text{zeros of }F\text{ inside }\gamma,\ \text{with multiplicity}).$$ No poles anywhere — nothing to biject, nothing to cancel.

Step 1 — the hypothesis makes both functions admissible. On $\gamma^\ast$, $|f|>|g|\ge 0$ gives $f\neq 0$, and $|f+g|\ge |f|-|g|>0$ gives $f+g\neq 0$. So the argument principle applies to each of $f$ and $f+g$:

$$N_f=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f'}{f}\,dz,\qquad N_{f+g}=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{(f+g)'}{f+g}\,dz.$$

Step 2 — the log-derivative identity, on the contour only. Wherever $f\neq 0$ and $f+g\neq 0$ — in particular at every point of $\gamma^\ast$ — the quotient rule on $h=(f+g)/f$ gives

$$\frac{h'}{h}\;=\;\frac{(f+g)'}{f+g}\;-\;\frac{f'}{f}.$$

This is just $\big(\log(f+g)-\log f\big)' $; it needs nothing about the interior of $\gamma$. Subtracting the two integrals in Step 1 and using it on $\gamma^\ast$:

$$N_{f+g}-N_f \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\!\left(\frac{(f+g)'}{f+g}-\frac{f'}{f}\right)dz \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{h'}{h}\,dz.$$

Step 3 — the winding-number argument (unchanged from the notes), applied to $h$ directly. On $\gamma^\ast$, $|g/f|<1$, so $\Gamma=h\circ\gamma$ has image in $B(1,1)\subset\{\operatorname{Re}>0\}$. The principal branch $\operatorname{Log}$ is holomorphic there and is a primitive of $1/w$, and $\Gamma$ is closed ($\Gamma(1)=\Gamma(0)$), so

$$\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{h'}{h}\,dz \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\Gamma \frac{dw}{w} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\big(\operatorname{Log}\Gamma(1)-\operatorname{Log}\Gamma(0)\big)\;=\;0.$$
$$\boxed{\,N_{f+g}-N_f = 0.\,}$$

Why this has no gap: the argument principle is only ever used on $f$ and $f+g$, each a holomorphic function with no zeros on $\gamma^\ast$, so each integral is unambiguously a zero-count — the zeros of $f$ and of $f+g$ are counted independently, coincidences and all. The function $h$ enters solely as the ratio whose log-derivative is the difference of the two integrands on the contour. We never decompose $h$ into zeros and poles, never invoke net order, and never need the false converse.

Check on $g=f/2$. Then $h\equiv\tfrac32$, so $h'/h\equiv 0$ and the middle integral is literally $0$; meanwhile $N_{f+g}-N_f = k-k = 0$ at each shared zero. The two sides agree without ever asking whether a shared zero is "a zero or a pole of $h$" — the question never comes up.
