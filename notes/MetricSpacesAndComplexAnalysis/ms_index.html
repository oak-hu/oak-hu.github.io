0  The big picture

Three storylines, all converging on the parallel complex analysis course. (1) Calculus: cts partials ⇒ differentiable → Inverse Function Theorem, whose proof engine is the Contraction Mapping Theorem. (2) Topology of metric spaces: open sets → continuity via preimages → closure-by-sequences → completeness / connectedness / compactness, each with its own equivalence theorem. (3) Geometry of $\mathbb C_\infty$: Möbius maps and conformality. Red arrows = the load-bearing chains.

1  Differentiability in $\mathbb R^2$

DefTotal derivative (1.1.1)
$f:\Omega\subseteq\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^m$ is differentiable at $a$ if there is a linear map $L:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^m$ with $$\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)-Lh}{|h|}=0;$$ then $L=df(a)$, with matrix the Jacobian $(f_x\;f_y)$ (in coords; eq. 1.5) — which also proves $L$ is unique.
  • Workhorse rearrangement (1.2): $f(a+h)-f(a)=Lh+R(h)|h|$, $R(h)\to0$ — "remainder vanishes faster than linearly" (first-order Taylor, tangent plane).
  • Why divide by $|h|$, not $h$? You can't divide by a vector — except in $\mathbb R^2\cong\mathbb C$, where dividing by $h$ makes sense and gives a much stronger condition: "the whole subject of complex analysis."
  • For $n=1$ this recovers the usual derivative; for $n\ge2$ the difference-quotient definition is "wholly inadequate" since $h/|h|$ has no limit.
DefDirectional derivatives (1.2.1)
$\partial_n f(a)=\lim_{\lambda\to0}\frac{f(a+\lambda n)-f(a)}{\lambda}$ for unit $n$. If $f$ is differentiable at $a$ then all directional derivatives exist and $\partial_nf(a)=Ln$ (eq. 1.4). Codomain dimension is irrelevant (work componentwise, WLOG $m=1$).
  • "Million dollar question": all directional derivatives exist ⇒ differentiable? No (Ex 1.4.2 below) — but Thm 1.3.1 is the usable partial converse.
ThmCts partials ⇒ differentiable (1.3.1)
Hyp: $f:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R$ with partial derivatives that exist and are cts (works on any open $\Omega$ — openness ensures the MVT segments below stay in the domain).
Then: $f$ is differentiable, $df(a)=(f_x(a),f_y(a))$.
Sketch
  • Telescope one coordinate at a time: $f(a{+}h,b{+}k)-f(a,b)=\big(f(a{+}h,b{+}k)-f(a{+}h,b)\big)+\big(f(a{+}h,b)-f(a,b)\big)$.
  • 1-variable MVT on each bracket: $=f_y(a{+}h,\,b{+}\theta_k k)\,k+f_x(a{+}\theta_h h,\,b)\,h$.
  • Subtract $Lh = f_x(a,b)h+f_y(a,b)k$: the error is a dot product of (partial-derivative differences) with $(h,k)$; Cauchy–Schwarz bounds it by $\sqrt{(\Delta f_x)^2+(\Delta f_y)^2}\cdot|(h,k)|$.
  • The $|(h,k)|$ cancels the denominator; continuity of the partials kills the $\Delta$'s as $(h,k)\to0$.
  • This is the theorem the CA notes import to prove "CR + cts partials ⇒ holomorphic" (CA Thm 1.2.7).
WarnThe Ch 1 counterexample zoo
  • Ex 1.4.2: $f=\frac{xy(x+y)}{x^2+y^2}$, $f(0)=0$ — homogeneous of degree 1, so every directional derivative at $0$ exists (equals $f(n)$); but $n\mapsto f(n)$ is not linear, so no total derivative.
  • Directional derivatives can all exist while $f$ isn't even cts (sheet 1).
  • $f_{xy}=f_{yx}$ can fail without continuity of second partials (sheet 1).
  • Differentiable ⇒ cts is true (Thm 1.5.2, immediate from (1.2)); chain rule $dh(a)=dg(f(a))\,df(a)$ holds (Thm 1.5.3, proof non-exam — needs matrix norms).
Def§1.6 Topology starter-pack (informal, for CA; redone rigorously later)
  • Open / closed / open nbhd (1.6.1 → Ch 5); path, path-connected (1.6.2 → §8.2); homotopic paths (1.6.3); simply-connected = p.c. + any two paths with common endpoints homotopic (1.6.4); connected (1.6.7 → §8.1); compact via open covers (1.6.10 → Ch 10); bounded (1.6.11).
  • $B(0,1)$ is simply-connected; the annulus $B(0,3)\setminus\bar B(0,1)$ is not (Ex 1.6.5).
  • Compactness mantra: "a good proxy for finiteness" — every open cover has a finite subcover.
  • Thm 1.6.12 (boundedness/attainment on compact sets) is proved by forward reference: compact ⇒ seq cpt (10.2.1) ⇒ image closed+bdd (9.3.1, 9.2.1) ⇒ sup attained (6.2.5).
  • §1.6.3 teases metrics: $d_\infty$ ("square balls") defines the same open sets as $d_2$; the "library metric" $\delta$ is a genuine metric that's geometrically useless. Moral: topology only sees the open sets.
ThmInverse Function Theorem in $\mathbb R^2$ (1.7.1) proof non-exam
Hyp: $\Omega\subseteq\mathbb R^2$ open; $f\in C^1(\Omega,\mathbb R^2)$; $df(a)$ invertible.
Then: there's an open $U\ni a$ with $f(U)$ open, $f:U\to f(U)$ a bijection, $f^{-1}\in C^1$, and $df^{-1}(f(a))=df(a)^{-1}$. ("Locally invert nonlinear maps the way you globally invert invertible linear ones.")
Sketch (1-d model proof via contraction mapping — chosen because it generalizes + is a numerical algorithm)
  • Normalize $a=0$, $f(0)=0$, $f'(0)=1$; set $\psi(x)=x-f(x)$, so $\psi'(0)=0$; continuity of $\psi'$ gives $|\psi'|\le\frac12$ on $[-r,r]$, hence (MVT) $|\psi(x)|\le\frac12|x|$.
  • To solve $f(x)=v_0$ with $|v_0|\le r/2$: the map $\phi(x)=x-f(x)+v_0$ sends $\bar B(0,r)$ to itself and is a contraction (constant $\frac12$).
  • Unique fixed point $x_\infty$ (= Thm 7.4.2), i.e. $f(x_\infty)=v_0$; iterating $\phi$ from any start computes it.
  • The only missing ingredient in 2-d: a notion of size for matrices to make "$|d\psi|\le\frac12$ on a disc" precise (Ex 1.7.3 demos the iteration numerically; it converges for $(0.5,0.5)$ and breaks somewhere past $(0.79,0.79)$ — "a challenge to the reader").
  • Worked use (Ex 1.7.2): $u=x^3y+y^2,\ v=\ln(x+y)$ is locally uniquely solvable for $(x,y)$ near $(6,\ln 3)\mapsto(1,2)$: Jacobian cts and invertible there.

2  Metric spaces

DefMetric space (2.2.1, 2.2.2)
$d:X\times X\to\mathbb R$ with (i) $d(x,y)\ge0$, $=0$ iff $x=y$; (ii) $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$; (iii) $d(x,z)\le d(x,y)+d(y,z)$. Reverse triangle inequality (2.2.2): $|d(x,y)-d(x,z)|\le d(y,z)$.
  • Motivation: the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition of continuity uses only distance — so axiomatize distance and continuity comes along. The axioms are minimal yet sufficient: that's the point.
  • One set, many metrics — always know which $d$ you're using.
ExThe example zoo (2.3.1–2.3.9)
  • $\mathbb R$ with $|x-y|$; $\mathbb R^n$ with $d_1=\sum|v_i-w_i|$, $d_2$ (Euclidean), $d_\infty=\max$ — only $d_2$'s triangle inequality needs work: it's $\|x+y\|_2\le\|x\|_2+\|y\|_2$ (2.3.3), proved by squaring + Cauchy–Schwarz.
  • Discrete metric (2.3.4): $d=1$ for $x\ne y$. "Routine and trivial," but the counterexample factory: not from a norm; $B(a,1)=\{a\}$ but $\bar B(a,1)=X$; every set open.
  • 2-adic (2.3.5): $d(x,y)=2^{-m}$, $2^m\|\,x-y$. Satisfies the stronger ultrametric inequality $d(x,z)\le\max(d(x,y),d(y,z))$. Intuition-breaker: $d(999,1000)=1\gt\frac18=d(0,1000)$.
  • Path metric on a connected graph (2.3.6); word metric on a group (2.3.7, = path metric on the Cayley graph); Hamming distance on $\{0,1\}^n$ (2.3.8, = restriction of $d_1$); projective space $\mathbf P(\mathbb R^n)$ with $d(L_1,L_2)=\sin\theta$ (2.3.9, well-defined by Cauchy–Schwarz).
DefNorms; $\ell^p$ spaces; products; subspaces (2.4.1–2.5.1)
  • Norm on a real vector space: $\|x\|=0\iff x=0$; $\|\lambda x\|=|\lambda|\|x\|$; triangle. Every norm gives a metric $d=\|x-y\|$ (2.4.2); the converse fails badly — norm-metrics are translation- and scaling-invariant, and any metric with both properties does come from a norm ($\|v\|=d(v,0)$, exercise).
  • $\ell^p$ sequence spaces (2.4.3): $\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell_\infty$ with $\sum|x_n|$, $(\sum x_n^2)^{1/2}$, $\sup|x_n|$; $\ell_2$ is an inner-product space — Hilbert space.
  • Subspace: restrict $d$ to any $Y\subseteq X$ ("subspace" ≠ vector subspace!).
  • Product (2.5.1): $d_{X\times Y}=\sqrt{d_X^2+d_Y^2}$, so $\mathbb R\times\mathbb R=\mathbb R^2$ Euclidean. Triangle inequality again via Cauchy–Schwarz. (max or sum give equivalent metrics — §4.3.)
Def+LemBalls; bounded (2.6.1–2.6.3)
$B(a,\varepsilon)=\{d(x,a)\lt\varepsilon\}$, $\bar B(a,\varepsilon)=\{d(x,a)\le\varepsilon\}$. $Y$ bounded = contained in some open ball $\iff$ some closed ball $\iff$ $\{d(y_1,y_2)\}$ bounded in $\mathbb R$ (2.6.3 — third⇒first: any centre $a\in Y$ works, radius $K+1$).
  • Caution: "closed ball = open ball + boundary" is false in general (discrete metric again).

3  Limits, continuity, function spaces

DefLimits; continuity; uniform continuity (3.1.1–3.1.3)
  • $x_n\to x$: $\forall\varepsilon\,\exists N$: $d(x_n,x)\lt\varepsilon$ for $n\ge N$. Limits are unique (triangle inequality with $\varepsilon=\frac12 d(a,b)$); may not exist ($0,1,0,1,\dots$).
  • Cts at $a$: $\forall\varepsilon\,\exists\delta$: $d_X(a,x)\lt\delta\Rightarrow d_Y(f(a),f(x))\lt\varepsilon$. Uniformly cts: one $\delta(\varepsilon)$ works for all base points.
LemmaSequential characterisation of continuity (3.1.4)
$f$ cts at $a$ $\iff$ for every sequence $x_n\to a$ we have $f(x_n)\to f(a)$.
Sketch
  • (⇒) chain the definitions. (⇐) contrapositive: failure of continuity gives an $\varepsilon$ such that $\delta=1/n$ produces $x_n\to a$ with $d(f(x_n),f(a))\ge\varepsilon$.
DefFunction spaces $B(X)$, $C(X)$, $C_b(X)$ (3.2.1–3.2.6)
  • $B(X)$ = bounded $f:X\to\mathbb R$ ($X$ any set) with the sup norm $\|f\|_\infty=\sup_X|f|$ — a normed vector space (3.2.2).
  • $C(X)$ = cts $f:X\to\mathbb R$ ($X$ a metric space) — a vector space via the $\varepsilon/2$ argument (3.2.4).
  • $C_b(X)=C(X)\cap B(X)$ with $d_\infty(f,g)=\|f-g\|_\infty$. Neither $C\subseteq B$ nor $B\subseteq C$ in general; $C[0,1]=C_b[0,1]$ since cts on $[0,1]$ ⇒ bounded (Prelims; later: compactness).
  • Ex 3.2.6: convergence in $d_\infty$ is uniform convergence — "essentially a tautology, but it takes a little thought to unravel the definitions."

4  Isometries, homeomorphisms, equivalence

DefIsometry (4.1.1)
$f:X\to Y$ with $d_Y(f(x),f(y))=d_X(x,y)$. Automatically injective; not automatically surjective — the right-shift on $\ell^2$, $(x_1,x_2,\dots)\mapsto(0,x_1,x_2,\dots)$, is an isometry onto a proper subspace. (Contrast: isometries of $\mathbb R^n$ are $x\mapsto Ax+b$, $A$ orthogonal — Prelims Geometry — hence surjective.) Bijective isometries of $X$ form the group $\operatorname{Isom}(X)$.
DefHomeomorphism (4.2.1–4.2.4)
cts bijection whose inverse is also cts.
  • The inverse clause has teeth: $f:[0,1)\cup[2,3]\to[0,2]$ ($x\mapsto x$ resp. $x-1$) is a cts bijection whose inverse jumps at $1$ — not a homeomorphism.
  • Homeomorphic pairs: closed disc ≅ square (radial stretch); $(-1,1)\cong\mathbb R$ via $x/(1-|x|)$; all of $\mathbb R$, $(a,b)$, $(a,\infty)$, $(-\infty,a)$ mutually homeomorphic; coffee cup ≅ doughnut.
  • Moral for later: completeness is not preserved by homeomorphism ($(0,1)\cong\mathbb R$); connectedness and (seq) compactness are.
PropEquivalent metrics (4.3.1, 4.3.2) non-exam
$d,d'$ on $X$ are equivalent if $\operatorname{id}:(X,d)\to(X,d')$ is a homeomorphism (⟺ balls of each metric nest inside balls of the other); then limits/continuity/open sets coincide. Strongly equivalent: $d\le Cd'$ and $d'\le Cd$ (strictly stronger). Prop 4.3.2: $d_1,d_2,d_\infty$ on $\mathbb R^n$ are (strongly) equivalent: $\|x\|_\infty\le\|x\|_1\le n\|x\|_\infty$, $\|x\|_\infty\le\|x\|_2\le\sqrt n\,\|x\|_\infty$.

5  Open and closed sets

DefOpen; closed (5.1.1–5.1.4)
  • $U$ open in $X$: every $y\in U$ has $B(y,\delta)\subseteq U$. $F$ closed: $F^c$ open.
  • Open balls are open (5.1.2 — not a tautology: pick $\varepsilon'$ with $d(x,a)\lt\varepsilon-\varepsilon'$, triangle inequality); closed balls are closed (5.1.4, same trick with the reverse triangle inequality); singletons closed.
  • Closed ≠ not-open: $\mathbb R$ is both; $(0,1)$ open only; $[0,1]$ closed only; $(0,1]$ neither.
  • In the discrete metric every set is open (and closed).
LemmaOpen-set axioms (5.2.1, 5.2.2)
$X,\emptyset$ open; arbitrary unions of opens are open; finite intersections of opens are open. Dually for closed sets (de Morgan): arbitrary intersections, finite unions.
  • Finiteness essential: $\bigcap_n(-\frac1n,\frac1n)=\{0\}$, not open. (Take $\varepsilon=\min\varepsilon_i$ — only positive for finite $I$.)
  • These three properties define a topological space (§5.4 non-exam): a set + a declared family of "open sets" satisfying them, no metric needed. Metric spaces are topological spaces (5.4.1); some topological spaces are not metrizable; continuity there is defined by Def 5.4.2 = preimage condition, with no $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ available.
PropContinuity ⟺ preimages of opens are open (5.3.1)
$f:X\to Y$ is cts $\iff$ $f^{-1}(U)$ is open in $X$ for every open $U\subseteq Y$ $\iff$ preimages of closed sets are closed.
Sketch
  • (⇒) for $a\in f^{-1}(U)$, a ball $B(f(a),\varepsilon)\subseteq U$ exists; continuity converts it into $B(a,\delta)\subseteq f^{-1}(U)$.
  • (⇐) apply the hypothesis to the open ball $B(f(a),\varepsilon)$ — that is continuity at $a$.
  • What it does NOT say: cts maps needn't send opens to opens — even injectively: $t\mapsto e^{2\pi it}$, $[0,1)\to S^1$, sends the open set $[0,\frac12)$ to a non-open arc. (Cf. the open mapping theorem in CA for how special holomorphic maps are.)
LemmaSubspace topology (5.5.1)
For $Y\subseteq X$: $U$ open in $Y$ $\iff$ $U=Y\cap V$, $V$ open in $X$ (and same for closed). Proof: $B_Y(y,r)=Y\cap B_X(y,r)$; for (⇒) take $V=\bigcup_y B_X(y,\varepsilon_y)$.
  • The classic confuser: $(0,1]$ is not open in $\mathbb R$ but IS open in $Y=(0,1]\cup[2,3]$ (e.g. $B_Y(1,\frac12)=(\frac12,1]$). This example powers the disconnection of $[0,1]\cup[2,3]$ in Ch 8.
  • Always say open in what.

6  Interiors, closures, limit points

DefInterior, closure, boundary, dense (6.1.1–6.1.3)
  • $\operatorname{int}(S)$ = union of all opens inside $S$ = largest such; $\bar S$ = intersection of all closeds containing $S$ = smallest such; $\partial S=\bar S\setminus\operatorname{int}(S)$; $S$ dense if $\bar S=X$. (Open/closed taken in $X$, not in the subspace — else trivial.)
  • $\operatorname{int}[a,b]=(a,b)$; $\operatorname{int}\mathbb Q=\emptyset$; $\mathbb Q$ and the dyadic rationals are dense in $\mathbb R$.
Lem+CorClosure via balls and sequences — the workhorse (6.1.4, 6.1.5)
  • 6.1.4: $a\in\bar S$ $\iff$ every ball $B(a,\varepsilon)$ meets $S$ (so: dense ⟺ meets every open set).
  • 6.1.5: $a\in\bar S$ $\iff$ some sequence in $S$ converges to $a$. Hence: $S$ closed ⟺ closed under limits of its sequences.
Sketch
  • 6.1.4: if a ball misses $S$, its complement is a closed set ⊇ $S$ but ∌ $a$; conversely $\bar S^c$ is open.
  • 6.1.5: pick $x_n\in B(a,\frac1n)\cap S$ (this innocuous step is where the notes flag the axiom of countable choice, §2.1).
  • Used constantly in Ch 7 & 9 (7.2.1, 7.2.3, 7.3.2, 9.2.1).
  • Caution (6.1.6): $\overline{B(a,\varepsilon)}\subseteq\bar B(a,\varepsilon)$ can be strict — discrete metric: $\overline{B(x,1)}=\{x\}$ but $\bar B(x,1)=X$.
DefLimit points (6.2.1–6.2.5) ("cultural reference"; not used later)
  • $a\in L(S)$: every ball about $a$ meets $S\setminus\{a\}$. Points of $S$ not in $L(S)$ = isolated points. Ex: $S=(0,1]\cup\{2\}$ ⇒ $L(S)=[0,1]$ — note $0\notin S$ is a limit point, $2\in S$ isn't.
  • $L(S)$ is closed (6.2.3); $\bar S=S\cup L(S)$ (6.2.4); $S$ closed ⟺ $L(S)\subseteq S$ (6.2.5).

7  Completeness

DefCauchy; complete (7.1.1–7.1.3)
  • Cauchy: $\forall\varepsilon\,\exists N$: $d(x_n,x_m)\lt\varepsilon$ for $n,m\ge N$. Complete: every Cauchy sequence converges (in the space!).
  • 7.1.2: convergent ⇒ Cauchy ⇒ bounded; both converses fail: $1/n$ in $(0,1]$ is Cauchy, not convergent there; $1,\frac12,1,\frac12,\dots$ is bounded, not Cauchy.
  • $\mathbb R$ complete (Prelims), hence $\mathbb R^n$ (coordinatewise). $(0,1)$ incomplete yet $\cong\mathbb R$: completeness is not a topological invariant.
LemmaComplete ⟺ closed (inside a complete space) (7.2.1)
Hyp: $X$ complete, $Y\subseteq X$ a subspace.
Then: $Y$ complete $\iff$ $Y$ closed in $X$.
Sketch
  • Both directions are Cor 6.1.5 + uniqueness of limits: closed traps the limit in $Y$; complete forces sequence limits into $Y$.
LemmaCantor's intersection theorem (7.2.2, 7.2.3)
Hyp: $X$ complete; $S_1\supseteq S_2\supseteq\cdots$ non-empty closed, with $\operatorname{diam}(S_n)\to0$ (diam = $\sup$ of pairwise distances, 7.2.2).
Then: $\bigcap_n S_n$ = exactly one point.
Sketch
  • Pick $x_n\in S_n$: nesting + shrinking diameters make $(x_n)$ Cauchy; the limit lies in every (closed) $S_i$ by 6.1.5; uniqueness since $d(a,b)\le\operatorname{diam}(S_i)\to0$.
  • Drop $\operatorname{diam}\to0$: uniqueness fails, and the intersection can even be empty — $S_i=[i,\infty)$. (Compare Lemma 10.2.2: compactness instead rescues non-emptiness.)
Thm$B(X)$ and $C_b(X)$ are complete (7.3.1, 7.3.2)
Hyp: 7.3.1: $X$ any set. 7.3.2: $X$ a metric space.
Sketch
  • $B(X)$: Cauchy in $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ ⇒ pointwise Cauchy ⇒ pointwise limit $f$ ($\mathbb R$ complete); freeze $n$, let $m\to\infty$ in the Cauchy bound to upgrade pointwise → sup-norm convergence; boundedness from the $\varepsilon=1$ tail.
  • $C_b(X)$: by 7.2.1 just show $C_b(X)$ is closed in $B(X)$; by 6.1.5 that means: uniform limit of cts is cts — the classic $\varepsilon/3$ argument: $|f(x)-f(a)|\le|f(x)-f_n(x)|+|f_n(x)-f_n(a)|+|f_n(a)-f(a)|$.
  • This is the metric-space rerun of Prelims "uniform limit of cts functions is cts"; in CA it's the analogue of Prop 3.3.12 territory (locally uniform limits).
ThmContraction Mapping Theorem (7.4.1, 7.4.2)
Hyp:
  • $X$ non-empty (the notes: "many people have lost a mark for forgetting this") and complete;
  • $f:X\to X$ a contraction: $\exists K\in[0,1)$ with $d(f(x),f(y))\le K\,d(x,y)$ — a uniform $K\lt1$, strictly stronger than "$d(f(x),f(y))\lt d(x,y)$".
Then: $f$ has a unique fixed point.
Sketch (constructive — the proof is an algorithm)
  • Uniqueness: two fixed points give $d(x_1,x_2)\le K\,d(x_1,x_2)$ ⇒ $d=0$.
  • Existence: iterate $x_{n+1}=f(x_n)$ from any $x_0$. Induction: $d(x_n,x_{n-1})\le K^{n-1}d(x_0,x_1)$.
  • Triangle + geometric series: $d(x_n,x_m)\le\frac{K^m}{1-K}\,d(x_0,x_1)\to0$ ⇒ Cauchy ⇒ (completeness) $x_n\to x$.
  • $f$ cts (Lipschitz, 7.4.1) ⇒ $f(x)=\lim f(x_n)=\lim x_{n+1}=x$.
  • Hypotheses are sharp: $f(x)=x+\frac1x$ on $[1,\infty)$ satisfies $d(f(x),f(y))\lt d(x,y)$, no fixed point (no uniform $K$); $f(x)=x/2$ on $(0,1)$ is a genuine contraction, no fixed point (incomplete space).
  • Applications: the IFT proof engine; Picard's theorem for ODEs (course A1).

8  Connectedness and path-connectedness

Def(Dis)connected (8.1.1)
Disconnected: $X=U\sqcup V$ with $U,V$ non-empty open; connected = not. Ex: $[0,1]\cup[2,3]$ is disconnected — both halves are open in the subspace (Lemma 5.5.1's example doing real work).
LemmaClopen criterion + subspace criterion (8.1.2, 8.1.3)
8.1.2, TFAE: (i) $X$ connected; (ii) every cts $f:X\to\{0,1\}$ is constant; (iii) the only clopen sets are $\emptyset, X$. 8.1.3: $Y\subseteq X$ is connected iff whenever $Y\subseteq U\cup V$ ($U,V$ open in $X$) with $U\cap V\cap Y=\emptyset$, then $Y\subseteq U$ or $Y\subseteq V$.
Sketch
  • (i)⇒(ii): $f^{-1}(0), f^{-1}(1)$ are disjoint opens covering $X$. (ii)⇒(iii): characteristic function of a clopen set is cts. (iii)⇒(i): a disconnection makes $U$ clopen.
  • 8.1.3 is just 8.1.2 + subspace opens (5.5.1).
  • The {0,1}-criterion is the CA identity-theorem move ("open and closed in a domain ⇒ all or nothing") in its native habitat.
LemmaBuilding connected sets (8.1.4–8.1.7)
  • Sunflower lemma (8.1.4): connected sets with a common point have connected union. Sketch: a cts $f\to\{0,1\}$ is constant on each petal, and they share the centre's value.
  • Closures (8.1.5): $A$ connected, $A\subseteq B\subseteq\bar A$ ⇒ $B$ connected. Sketch: via 8.1.3; $A\subseteq U$ forces $\bar A\subseteq\overline{V^c}=V^c$, so $B\subseteq U$. (This is what makes the sine curve connected.)
  • Cts images (8.1.6): $X$ connected ⇒ $f(X)$ connected (pull back a disconnection). So connectedness is a topological invariant — unlike completeness.
  • Components (8.1.7): maximal connected subsets partition $X$ (sunflower + maximality).
ThmIntervals are connected; IVT (8.1.8)
Hyp: $[x,y]\subset\mathbb R$ (any interval works).
Sketch (the sup argument)
  • Suppose $[x,y]\subseteq U\cup V$, disjoint on $[x,y]$, $x\in U$, $y\in V$ (careful WLOG: shrink the interval so its endpoints witness both sets).
  • Let $c=\sup S$ where $S=[x,y]\cap U$ — exists by completeness of $\mathbb R$ (sup axiom).
  • $c\in U$: openness gives $[c,c+\varepsilon)\subseteq U$, beating the sup. $c\in V$: openness gives $(c-\varepsilon,c]\subseteq V$, so $c-\varepsilon/2$ is a smaller upper bound. Both impossible.
  • IVT falls out: $f([a,b])$ is connected (8.1.6), hence an interval, hence contains everything between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$.
DefPath-connectedness (8.2.1, 8.2.2)
$X$ is p.c. if any $a,b$ are joined by a path (cts $\gamma:[0,1]\to X$). "Joined by a path" is an equivalence relation (constant path / reversal $\gamma(1-t)$ / concatenation $\gamma_1\star\gamma_2$); classes = path-components.
ThmPath-connected ⇒ connected (8.3.1)
Sketch
  • Take cts $f:X\to\{0,1\}$ and any $a,b$; the composite $f\circ\gamma:[0,1]\to\{0,1\}$ is cts on the connected $[0,1]$ (Thm 8.1.8) ⇒ constant ⇒ $f(a)=f(b)$ ⇒ $f$ constant ⇒ connected (8.1.2).
ThmConnected open in a normed space ⇒ path-connected (8.3.2)
Hyp: $X$ connected, open, in a normed space (all three needed!).
Sketch
  • Path-components are open: around $a$ take $B(a,\varepsilon)\subseteq X$ (openness); the straight segment $(1-t)a+tb$ stays in the ball (norm structure: $\|\gamma(t)-a\|\le\|a-b\|\lt\varepsilon$), so the whole ball joins to $a$.
  • Path-components partition $X$ into opens; more than one would disconnect $X$ ⇒ exactly one.
  • The CA payoff: for open $U\subseteq\mathbb C$, connected ⟺ path-connected — this is why "domain" in complex analysis can mean either.
ThmTopologist's sine curve: connected ⇏ path-connected (8.3.3) proof non-exam
Sketch
  • Connected: the curve part $E$ is the cts image of $(0,1]$ ⇒ connected (8.1.6 + 8.1.8); $A=\bar E$ ⇒ connected by the closure lemma 8.1.5.
  • Not p.c.: suppose $\gamma$ runs from $(0,0)$ to $(1,\sin1)$. Let $t=\sup\gamma^{-1}(\ell)$ ($\ell$ = the segment; closed, so $\gamma(t)\in\ell$, and $\gamma(u)\in E$ for $u\gt t$).
  • Continuity of $y\circ\gamma$ at $t$: on $[t,t+\delta]$ the $y$-coordinate varies by $\le1$ (eq. 8.1).
  • But $x\circ\gamma$ is cts with $x(\gamma(t))=0\lt x(\gamma(t+\delta))$, so by the IVT it sweeps an interval $[0,c]$ — and on $(0,c]$, $\sin\frac1x$ hits both $\pm1$ infinitely often. Contradiction.

9  Sequential compactness

DefSequentially compact (9.1.1, 9.1.2)
Every sequence in $X$ has a subsequence converging in $X$.
  • $[0,1]$ ✓ (Bolzano–Weierstrass). $(0,1)$ ✗ ($1/n$); $\mathbb Q\cap[0,1]$ ✗ (decimal truncations of $\pi-3$); $\mathbb R$ ✗ ($x_n=n$).
LemmasFirst properties (9.2.1–9.3.2)
  • 9.2.1: seq cpt subspace ⇒ closed and bounded. Sketch: not closed ⇒ a sequence escapes to a boundary point (6.1.5), all subsequences follow; unbounded ⇒ pick $d(y_0,y_n)\ge n$, no subsequence can settle. Converse false: $(0,1)$ inside itself.
  • 9.2.2: closed subset of seq cpt is seq cpt.
  • 9.3.1: cts image of seq cpt is seq cpt ($f(x_{n_k})\to f(a)$ by continuity). Hence seq cpt is a homeomorphism invariant, and:
  • EVT: cts $f:X\to\mathbb R$ on seq cpt $X$ has closed bounded image ⇒ bounded and attains its bounds.
  • 9.3.2: cts on seq cpt ⇒ uniformly cts. Sketch: if not, get $a_n,b_n$ with $d(a_n,b_n)\lt\frac1n$ but $|f(a_n)-f(b_n)|\ge\varepsilon$; pass to $a_{n_k}\to\ell$, drag $b$ along, contradict continuity at $\ell$ via $\varepsilon/2$.
Prop+CorProducts; Bolzano–Weierstrass in $\mathbb R^n$ (9.4.1–9.4.3)
9.4.2: a product of two seq cpt spaces is seq cpt. Cor 9.4.3 (B–W): closed + bounded in $\mathbb R^n$ ⇒ seq cpt.
Sketch
  • 9.4.1: convergence in $X\times Y$ = coordinatewise (projections are 1-Lipschitz).
  • 9.4.2: subsequence for the $x$'s, then a sub-subsequence for the $y$'s — "rather unpleasant triple subscripts," but the point is just: refine twice.
  • 9.4.3: bounded ⇒ inside a cube $[-M,M]^n$; B–W on $\mathbb R$ + products (iterated) make the cube seq cpt; closed subset of seq cpt is seq cpt (9.2.2).
ThmSeq compact ⟺ complete + totally bounded (9.5.1–9.5.3)
Defn (9.5.2): totally bounded = for every $\varepsilon\gt0$, finitely many $\varepsilon$-balls cover $X$.
Thm 9.5.3 ("one of the more substantial theorems of the course"): $X$ seq cpt $\iff$ $X$ complete and totally bounded.
Sketch
  • (⇒) complete: Cauchy + convergent subsequence ⇒ convergent (9.5.1). Totally bounded by greedy contradiction: if no finite $\varepsilon$-cover, greedily pick $x_{n+1}$ outside $\bigcup B(x_i,\varepsilon)$ — an $\varepsilon$-separated sequence has no Cauchy subsequence.
  • (⇐), the substantial direction: fix finite covers by balls of radius $2^{-m}$ for every $m$.
  • Pigeonhole cascade: some radius-1 ball holds infinitely many terms → subsequence $\sigma^{(0)}$; some radius-$\frac12$ ball holds infinitely many of those → $\sigma^{(1)}$; iterate.
  • Diagonal: $\sigma^*_i$ := $i$-th term of $\sigma^{(i)}$. For $n,m\ge N$ both terms lie in one ball of radius $2^{-N}$ ⇒ Cauchy ⇒ converges by completeness.
  • Note flagged in the notes: the argument shows any sequence in a totally bounded space has a Cauchy subsequence — completeness enters only at the last step.
  • Complete + bounded does NOT suffice (9.5.1): the moving bump — in $C_b(\mathbb R)$, $f_n=\phi(\cdot-n)$ (a tent of height 1 slid to position $n$) all live in $\bar B(0,1)$, which is complete (7.2.1 + 7.3.2) and bounded; but $\|f_n-f_m\|_\infty=1$ for $n\ne m$: no Cauchy subsequence. Total boundedness is the right strengthening; in $\mathbb R^n$ bounded ⟺ totally bounded (via 9.4.3).

10  Compactness

DefOpen covers; compactness (10.1.1–10.1.3)
$X$ is compact if every open cover $\{U_i\}_{i\in I}$ has a finite subcover. $\mathbb R$ is not: $\{(-n,n)\}_{n\in\mathbb N}$.
  • Motivation problem acknowledged head-on, with von Neumann: "in mathematics you don't understand things. You just get used to them." Why it's the right notion: (a) equivalent to seq cpt in metric spaces (this chapter); (b) it's the definition that survives into general topological spaces, where the equivalence fails.
  • For subspaces $Y\subseteq X$, covers by opens of $X$ — equivalent to the internal definition by Lemma 5.5.1.
PropCompact ⇒ sequentially compact (10.2.1, 10.2.2)
Sketch
  • Lemma 10.2.2: in a compact space, nested non-empty closed $S_1\supseteq S_2\supseteq\cdots$ have non-empty intersection — else the complements form an open cover with no finite subcover. (Compactness replaces the complete + diam→0 hypotheses of Cantor 7.2.3.)
  • Apply to closures of the tails $A_n=\{x_n,x_{n+1},\dots\}$: pick $a\in\bigcap\bar A_n$; since $a\in\bar A_{n_k+1}$, choose $x_{n_{k+1}}$ within $1/(k{+}1)$ of $a$ — a convergent subsequence.
PropSequentially compact ⇒ compact (10.3.1) proof non-exam
Sketch (direct nested-balls construction — these notes do not use a Lebesgue number)
  • Seq cpt ⇒ complete + totally bounded (9.5.3); fix finite covers by radius-$2^{-m}$ balls for each $m$.
  • If some open cover $\{U_i\}$ had no finite subcover: some radius-$\frac12$ ball $B_1$ is not finitely coverable; among radius-$\frac14$ balls meeting $B_1$, one ($B_2$) is not finitely coverable (else $B_1$ would be); iterate.
  • Centres: $d(x_m,x_{m+1})\lt2^{-m}+2^{-(m+1)}$ ⇒ geometric series ⇒ Cauchy ⇒ $x_n\to x$ (completeness).
  • $x\in U_1$ for some member; $B(x,\varepsilon)\subseteq U_1$; for large $n$, $B_n\subseteq B(x,\varepsilon)\subseteq U_1$ — so $B_n$ is finitely coverable (by one set!). Contradiction.
ThmThe equivalence theorem + Heine–Borel (10.3.2, 10.3.3)
  • 10.3.2 (TFAE, any metric space): compact $\iff$ seq compact $\iff$ complete + totally bounded.
  • 10.3.3 (Heine–Borel): $S\subseteq\mathbb R^n$ compact $\iff$ closed and bounded.
Sketch
  • 10.3.2 = 10.2.1 + 10.3.1 + 9.5.3, nothing more.
  • Heine–Borel: (⇒) compact ⇒ seq cpt ⇒ closed + bounded (9.2.1); (⇐) closed + bounded ⇒ seq cpt (B–W, 9.4.3) ⇒ compact (10.3.1).
  • Scope warning: "closed + bounded ⇒ compact" is an $\mathbb R^n$ fact, not a metric-space fact — the moving bump (9.5.1) is the standard counterexample in infinite dimensions.

11  Conformal maps and $\mathbb C_\infty$

Def+LemStereographic projection; the chordal metric; $\mathbb C_\infty$ (11.1.1–11.1.3)
  • Formula (11.1.1): $S(z)=\frac1{1+|z|^2}\big(2\operatorname{Re}z,\,2\operatorname{Im}z,\,|z|^2-1\big)$ (parametrize the line, solve for the sphere point).
  • Chordal metric = pull back $\mathbb R^3$ distance: $d(z,w)=\dfrac{2|z-w|}{\sqrt{1+|z|^2}\sqrt{1+|w|^2}}$ (11.1.2, via $\|S(z)-S(w)\|^2=2-2\langle S(z),S(w)\rangle$), and $d(z,\infty)=\dfrac2{\sqrt{1+|z|^2}}$ (11.1.3).
  • On $\mathbb C$ it's equivalent (same opens/limits — strongly so on bounded sets, not globally) to the usual metric, but it also makes sense of $\infty$: $\mathbb C_\infty=\mathbb C\cup\{\infty\}\cong\mathbb S$, and "$z_n\to\infty$" becomes literal metric convergence.
  • Translations, dilations, inversion are cts bijections of $\mathbb C_\infty$ (11.1.4–11.1.6): continuity at $\infty$/$0$ checked via the chordal formulas. Geometric gem: under $S$, inversion $z\mapsto1/z$ is just rotation of the sphere by $\pi$ about the $x$-axis.
  • Forward ref: $\mathbb C_\infty$ = first Riemann surface (Part B Geometry of Surfaces).
Def+PropMöbius maps (11.1.7–11.1.9)
For $g=\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix}\in\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb C)$: $\Psi_g(z)=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}$ on $\mathbb C_\infty$, with $\Psi_g(-d/c)=\infty$, $\Psi_g(\infty)=a/c$ (or $\infty\mapsto\infty$ if $c=0$). $\Psi_g=\Psi_{g'}$ iff $g=\lambda g'$.
  • 11.1.8: $\Psi_{g_1g_2}=\Psi_{g_1}\circ\Psi_{g_2}$ — a group action of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb C)$ on $\mathbb C_\infty$.
  • 11.1.9 (generators): every Möbius map = composition of translations $T(a)$, dilations $D(b)$, inversion $J$. Sketch: the matrix identity $g=T(\frac ac)\,D(\frac{bc-ad}c)\,J\,T(d)\,D(c)$ ($c\neq0$; the case $c=0$ is easier) — proving it at the matrix level handles the $\infty$ bookkeeping for free via 11.1.8.
PropCirclines map to circlines (11.1.10, 11.1.11) proof non-exam
Circline = circle in $\mathbb C$, or line $\cup\,\{\infty\}$ (lines: $|z-a|=|z-b|$). Möbius maps send circlines to circlines.
Sketch
  • By the generator decomposition (11.1.9), check translations/dilations (obvious) and inversion; the only interesting case: circles through $0$ ↔ lines not through $0$ under $z\mapsto1/z$.
Def+PropConformality; holo + $f'\neq0$ ⇒ conformal (11.2.1–11.2.6)
Defns: tangent vector of a $C^1$ path with $\gamma'\neq0$ (11.2.1); $T$ is conformal at $z_0$ if for every pair of $C^1$ paths through $z_0$, the angle between tangent vectors is preserved by $T\circ\gamma_i$ (11.2.2).
Prop 11.2.3: $f$ holo on open $U$, $f'(z_0)\neq0$ ⇒ $f$ conformal at $z_0$. Lemma 11.2.6: Möbius maps are conformal ($f'=\frac{ad-bc}{(cz+d)^2}\neq0$). Lemma 11.2.5 non-exam: stereographic projection is conformal (planes through $N$ cut the sphere in circles; symmetry swaps the angles at $N$ and at $S(z_0)$).
Sketch of 11.2.3 — why holomorphy is exactly angle-preservation
  • Chain rule for $\eta_i=f\circ\gamma_i$: split the difference quotient as $\frac{f(\gamma(h))-f(z_0)}{\gamma(h)-z_0}\cdot\frac{\gamma(h)-z_0}{h}$ (legal since $\gamma'(0)\neq0$ keeps $\gamma(h)\neq z_0$); the first factor $\to f'(z_0)$ by complex differentiability.
  • So $\eta_i'(0)=f'(z_0)\,\gamma_i'(0)$: writing $f'(z_0)=\rho e^{i\theta}$, both tangent vectors get rotated by the same $\theta$ (and scaled by $\rho$) — angle differences survive.
  • This is the geometric meaning of the CR equations: multiplication by a complex number = rotation + scaling (cf. CA Thm 1.2.2, proof 2).
  • $f'\neq0$ is necessary (Ex 11.2.4): $z^2$ at the origin doubles angles.
Prop3-point transitivity (11.2.7)
Hyp: $z_1,z_2,z_3$ and $w_1,w_2,w_3$ triples of pairwise distinct points of $\mathbb C_\infty$.
Then: there is a unique Möbius $f$ with $f(z_i)=w_i$. (Plausibility: 4 matrix entries minus rescaling = 3 complex degrees of freedom.)
Sketch
  • Reduce to mapping any triple to $(0,1,\infty)$, using the group structure (11.1.8): $f=f_2\circ f_1^{-1}$.
  • Existence: $f(z)=\dfrac{(z-z_1)(z_2-z_3)}{(z-z_3)(z_2-z_1)}$ (limit versions when some $z_i=\infty$).
  • Uniqueness: reduces to — a Möbius map fixing $0,1,\infty$ is the identity: $f(\infty)=\infty\Rightarrow c=0$; $f(0)=0\Rightarrow b=0$; $f(1)=1\Rightarrow a=d$.
ExUpper half-plane → unit disc (11.2.8)
  • Strategy: $\partial\mathbb H=\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}$ must go to the unit circle (circline → circline, 11.1.11); a circle is pinned by 3 points, so use 3-transitivity: send $0\mapsto-i$, $1\mapsto1$, $\infty\mapsto i$ ⇒ $f(z)=\frac{iz+1}{z+i}$.
  • Which side lands inside? Möbius maps are cts; the two components of $\mathbb C\setminus\mathbb R$ go to the two components of $\mathbb C\setminus S^1$ (connectedness!); test one point: $f(i)=0\in B(0,1)$ ⇒ $f(\mathbb H)=B(0,1)$.
  • Note how the chapter's three threads combine: circlines + transitivity + connectedness (Ch 8) in one worked example.
