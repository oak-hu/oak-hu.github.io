1.1–1.2  Holomorphy & the Cauchy–Riemann equations

DefOpen set, neighbourhood, domain (0.3.2, 0.3.3)
  • $U\subset\mathbb C$ is open if every $z\in U$ has a ball $B(z,\varepsilon)\subset U$.
  • A nbhd of $a$ = open set containing $a$ (for limits the notes relax this: $U$ just contains some $B(a,r)$).
  • A domain $D$ = connected open subset of $\mathbb C$. The open-vs-domain distinction matters all course long — watch for it below.
DefComplex differentiability; holomorphic (1.1.1, 1.1.4)
  • $f$ is differentiable at $a$ if $\displaystyle\lim_{z\to a}\frac{f(z)-f(a)}{z-a}$ exists; the limit is $f'(a)$. ($f:U\to\mathbb C$, $U$ open $\ni a$.)
  • $f$ is holo on $U$ ($U$ open) if differentiable at every point of $U$; holo at $a$ = holo on some open $U\ni a$.
  • Sum/product/quotient/chain rules hold exactly as in $\mathbb R$ (Lemma 1.1.2) — so polynomials are holo, with $\,(z^n)'=nz^{n-1}$.
LemmaLinear-approximation form (1.1.3)
$f$ differentiable at $a$ with derivative $f'(a)$ $\iff$ $$f(z)=f(a)+f'(a)(z-a)+\varepsilon(z)(z-a),\qquad \varepsilon(z)\to 0 \text{ as } z\to a.$$
  • The workhorse form. Used to prove the derivative rules, both proofs of CR, the converse 1.2.7, the chain rule for paths 2.1.3, and to kill the linear part in Goursat's bisection.
ThmCauchy–Riemann equations (1.2.2)
Hyp:
  • $U$ a nbhd of $z_0$, $f:U\to\mathbb C$ differentiable at the single point $z_0$;
  • $f=u+iv$ with $u,v$ real.
Then:
  • all four partials exist at $z_0$ and $$\partial_x u=\partial_y v,\qquad \partial_x v=-\partial_y u;$$
  • $f'(z_0)=\partial_x u+i\,\partial_x v$ (four equivalent expressions via CR).
Sketch
  • Proof 1: the difference-quotient limit is direction-independent. Approach along $z_0+h$ ($h$ real) → $f'=\partial_x u+i\partial_x v$; along $z_0+ih$ → $f'=-i\partial_y u+\partial_y v$. Equate, split Re/Im.
  • Proof 2: by 1.1.3, $f$ is real-differentiable with Jacobian = multiplication by $f'(z_0)=re^{i\theta}$, i.e. the rotation–scaling matrix $\begin{pmatrix} r\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta\\ r\sin\theta & r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$ — CR is exactly the statement that the Jacobian has this special form (real-linear vs complex-linear).
ThmConverse: CR + cts partials ⇒ holo (1.2.7)
Hyp:
  • $U\subseteq\mathbb C$ open; $f=(u,v):U\to\mathbb C$;
  • all four partials exist, are cts on $U$, and satisfy CR.
Then: $f$ is holo on $U$, $f'=\partial_x u+i\,\partial_x v$.
Sketch
  • Metric Spaces does the heavy lifting: cts partials ⇒ real differentiable, $f(z)=f(z_0)+L(z-z_0)+R(z)$ with $L$ the Jacobian.
  • CR ⇒ Jacobian $=\begin{pmatrix}\partial_x u & -\partial_x v\\ \partial_x v & \partial_x u\end{pmatrix}$ = multiplication by the complex number $\partial_x u+i\partial_x v$.
  • That is exactly the Lemma 1.1.3 form ⇒ complex differentiable.
  • Continuity of the partials is essential: $f=xy/(x^2+y^2)$, $f(0)=0$ satisfies CR at $0$ (all partials vanish) yet isn't even cts there (Ex 1.2.8).
WarnComplex differentiability is much stronger than real (1.2.4, 1.2.9)
  • $f(z)=\operatorname{Re} z$ (i.e. $u=x,v=0$): perfectly smooth as a real map, complex differentiable nowhere ($\partial_x u=1\ne 0=\partial_y v$).
  • If $f'(z_0)\ne0$ then $\overline{f(z)}$ and $f(\bar z)$ are not differentiable (at $z_0$, $\bar z_0$): conjugation flips CR — such functions are anti-holomorphic.
Lemma$f'\equiv 0$ on $\mathbb C$ ⇒ constant (1.2.10)
  • Via CR + 1-variable MVT: $u,v$ constant along horizontal and vertical lines.
  • Needed now for properties of $\exp$ (Prop 1.4.10); the proper version on domains is Cor 2.2.15.
DefWirtinger derivatives (1.2.11, 1.2.12) intuition only
  • $\partial_z=\tfrac12(\partial_x-i\partial_y)$, $\partial_{\bar z}=\tfrac12(\partial_x+i\partial_y)$.
  • CR $\iff \partial_{\bar z}f=0$, and then $f'=\partial_z f$. Slogan: holo = "depends on $z$, not on $\bar z$" (e.g. $|z|^2=z\bar z$: $\partial_{\bar z}=z\neq0$). The notes stress this is intuition, not usable in proofs.

1.3  Harmonic functions

DefLaplacian; harmonic; harmonic conjugates (1.3.1, 1.3.2)
  • $\Delta u=\partial_{xx}u+\partial_{yy}u$; $u$ (twice differentiable, on open $U\subseteq\mathbb R^2$) is harmonic if $\Delta u=0$.
  • $u,v$ harmonic with $u+iv$ holo are harmonic conjugates.
ThmRe/Im of holo are harmonic (1.3.3)
Hyp: $U$ open, $f=u+iv$ holo on $U$, and ($C^2$ assumption, later redundant since holo ⇒ $C^\infty$) $u,v$ twice cts differentiable.
Then: $u,v$ harmonic.
Sketch
  • CR twice: $\partial_{xx}u=\partial_{xy}v$, $\partial_{yy}u=-\partial_{yx}v$; symmetry of mixed partials kills the sum.
  • Wirtinger view: $\Delta=4\,\partial_z\partial_{\bar z}$.
  • non-exam On a s.c. domain every harmonic $u$ has a conjugate $v$, i.e. is locally $\operatorname{Re}$ of a holo function.

1.4  Power series, exp/trig, Log

DefRadius of convergence (1.4.1)
For $\sum a_nz^n$ with convergence set $S$: $\;R=\sup\{|z|:z\in S\}$ (or $\infty$).
PropConvergence disc + root formula (1.4.2)
  • $B(0,R)\subseteq S\subseteq \bar B(0,R)$; convergence is absolute on $B(0,R)$ and uniform on $\bar B(0,r)$ for every $r \lt R$ (boundary: anything can happen);
  • $\dfrac 1R=\limsup_n |a_n|^{1/n}$.
Sketch
  • Pick $|w|>r$ with $\sum a_nw^n$ convergent ⇒ terms bounded by $M$ ⇒ $|a_nz^n|\le M|r/w|^n$ on $\bar B(0,r)$ ⇒ Weierstrass M-test (the same comparison-with-geometric-series move that later powers Prop 2.5.6 and Laurent).
  • Root formula: $n$-th roots of $|a_nw^n|\le M$ give $\limsup\le 1/R$; conversely $|a_n|^{1/n}\le L'$ eventually ⇒ converges for $|z| \lt 1/L'$.
PropTerm-by-term differentiation (1.4.4; sums/products 1.4.3)
$\sum_{n\ge1} na_nz^{n-1}$ has the same radius $R$, and $s'(z)=\sum na_nz^{n-1}$ on $B(0,R)$. Hence power series are infinitely complex differentiable; sums and products of series work on $|z|\lt\min(R_1,R_2)$ (Cauchy product).
  • Consequence (Rmk 1.4.5): $f(z)=\sum a_n(z-z_0)^n$ has $a_n=f^{(n)}(z_0)/n!$ — such $f$ are called analytic, and analytic ⇒ holo. The course's headline (Cor 3.3.2) is the converse.
Exexp, cos, sin (1.4.6–1.4.11)
  • $e^z=\sum z^n/n!$, $\cos z=\frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}2$, $\sin z=\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i}$ — entire; $\exp'=\exp$, $\sin'=\cos$, $\cos'=-\sin$ (all by 1.4.4).
  • Prop 1.4.10: $e^{z+w}=e^ze^w$. Sketch: $f(z)=e^ze^{a-z}$ has $f'\equiv0$ ⇒ constant (Lemma 1.2.10) $=f(0)=e^a$.
  • Cor 1.4.11: $e^{x+iy}=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)$ ⇒ range of $\exp$ is $\mathbb C\setminus\{0\}$, and $\exp$ is $2\pi i$-periodic — not injective, which is the whole logarithm problem.
  • Not every real-$C^\infty$ function extends to $\mathbb C$; real-analytic ones do locally, e.g. $1/(1+x^2)\rightsquigarrow 1/(1+z^2)$ holo off $\pm i$ (Ex 1.4.7).
PropSimplified inverse function theorem (1.4.12)
Hyp: $f:U\to\mathbb C$ holo; $f$ one-to-one on some $V\ni z_0$; the inverse $g$ is assumed cts (this is the cheat — $\mathbb C$ has no order, so the Prelims monotonicity argument is unavailable; the honest version is Thm 3.6.8).
Then: $g$ differentiable at $w_0=f(z_0)$ with $g'(w_0)=1/f'(z_0)$.
Sketch
  • $\frac{g(w)-g(w_0)}{w-w_0}=\frac{z-z_0}{f(z)-f(z_0)}\to\frac1{f'(z_0)}$, where $z\to z_0$ because $g$ is cts.
PropPrincipal logarithm (1.4.14, 1.4.16, 1.4.21)
  • On $D=\mathbb C\setminus(-\infty,0]$, with $z=|z|e^{i\theta}$, $\theta\in(-\pi,\pi]$: $\;\operatorname{Log} z=\log|z|+i\theta$ is holo with $\operatorname{Log}'z=1/z$.
  • $\theta$ = principal value of the argument; principal power: $z^\alpha:=\exp(\alpha\operatorname{Log}z)$.
Sketch
  • Apply Prop 1.4.12: Log is an inverse of exp by construction; only need continuity.
  • $\log|z|$ obviously cts; for $\theta(z)$ use the law-of-cosines trick: $\cos(\theta(z+h)-\theta(z))=\frac{|z|^2+|z+h|^2-|h|^2}{2|z||z+h|}\to1$.
  • Then $\operatorname{Log}'(z)=1/\exp'(\operatorname{Log}z)=1/z$.
  • Removing $(-\infty,0]$ is essential: across the negative axis the argument jumps $\pi\rightsquigarrow-\pi$.
  • (Rmk 1.4.20 foreshadows: $\log z=\int_1^z dw/w$ — made rigorous by §2.5's cut-plane discussion.)
WarnLog and powers don't behave
  • $\operatorname{Log}(zw)\neq\operatorname{Log}z+\operatorname{Log}w$ in general: $\operatorname{Log}(e^{2\pi i/3})+\operatorname{Log}(e^{2\pi i/3})=\tfrac{4\pi i}3$ but $\operatorname{Log}(e^{4\pi i/3})=-\tfrac{2\pi i}3$.
  • $\sqrt{1/{-1}}=i\neq -i=\sqrt1/\sqrt{-1}$.

1.5  Branch cuts & multifunctions

DefMultifunction, branch, branch point, branch cut (1.5.1–1.5.3)
  • Multifunction on $U$: a map $f:U\to\mathcal P(\mathbb C)$ (each point ↦ a set of values), e.g. $[z^{1/2}]=\{w:w^2=z\}$, $[\log z]=\{w:e^w=z\}$.
  • Branch on $V\subseteq U$: a genuine function $g$ with $g(z)\in f(z)$; cts/holo branch if $g$ is.
  • $z_0$ is not a branch point if some punctured disc $D\setminus\{z_0\}$ admits a holo branch (footnote: any s.c. domain works — uses Cauchy later). Branch point at $\infty$: no holo branch on any $\{|z|>R\}$.
  • Branch cut: curve(s) whose complement admits a holo branch; must contain all branch points.
ExBranches of $[z^{1/2}]$ and $[\log z]$ (1.5.5–1.5.7)
  • $z=re^{i\theta}\Rightarrow$ the two roots are $\pm\sqrt re^{i\theta/2}$. Choosing $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$ gives a branch holo on $\mathbb C\setminus[0,\infty)$ (cut = positive axis); choosing $\theta\in(-\pi,\pi)$ gives the principal branch, cut $(-\infty,0]$.
  • $[\log z]=\{\log|z|+i(\theta+2\pi n)\}$; each cts choice of $\arg$ on a cut plane gives a holo branch (same proof as Prop 1.4.14).
  • $[z^\alpha]:=\exp(\alpha[\log z])$; each branch $L$ of log gives a branch $\exp(\alpha L)$ ("principal" = via Log).
PropMultifunction arithmetic — and its limits (1.5.8, 1.5.9)
As sets: $[\log z]+[\log w]=[\log zw]$ and $[z^\alpha][w^\alpha]=[(zw)^\alpha]$ (direct computation; $n+k$ ranges over all of $\mathbb Z$).
  • But $[z^\alpha][z^\beta]\neq[z^{\alpha+\beta}]$ in general: $[1^{1/2}][1^{1/2}]=\{\pm1\}\neq\{1\}=[1^1]$.
  • Exercise 1.5.10's bogus proof that $[\log(-z)]=[\log z]$: the flaw is that $2[\log z]\neq[\log z]+[\log z]$ — you can't cancel in set equations.
Ex$[(z^2-1)^{1/2}]$: a bounded branch cut (1.5.11–1.5.12)
  • Write $f(z)=|z-1|^{1/2}|z+1|^{1/2}e^{i\theta_1/2}e^{i\theta_2/2}$ ($\theta_i$ = args of $z\mp1$, cut along $(-\infty,0]$ each). Naively the cut is $(-\infty,1]$, but the cancellation above shrinks it to $[-1,1]$ — a bounded cut, so there's a holo branch near $\infty$.
  • Binomial series (1.5.11): the principal branch $f=\exp(\alpha\operatorname{Log}(1+z))$ equals $\sum_k\binom\alpha k z^k$ on $B(0,1)$. Sketch: both satisfy $(1+z)s'=\alpha s$; the ratio $g=s/f$ has $g'\equiv0$, $g(0)=1$ ⇒ $s=f$.

2.1–2.2  Paths, integration, FTC

DefPaths, reparametrization, oriented curves (2.1.1–2.1.7)
  • Path: cts $\gamma:[a,b]\to\mathbb C$; closed if $\gamma(a)=\gamma(b)$; image written $\gamma^*$.
  • $C^1$: $\gamma'$ exists and is cts; p.w.-$C^1$: finite concatenation of $C^1$ pieces (corners allowed). Opposite path $\gamma^-$; concatenation $\gamma_1\star\gamma_2$.
  • Equivalent paths: $\gamma_1=\gamma_2\circ s$ with $s$ a $C^1$ bijection, $s'>0$ (same direction). Equivalence classes = oriented curves.
  • Standard paths: circle $z_0+re^{2\pi it}$ (positive orientation), segment $[a,b]$, triangle $\gamma_{a,b,c}$.
  • Chain rule for paths (2.1.3, via the $\varepsilon$-form 1.1.3) underlies everything below. Caution (2.1.2): a $C^1$ path's image can still have a corner where $\gamma'=0$; the notes allow constant paths.
DefPath integral; length; arc-length integral (2.2.4, 2.2.6)
For $\gamma$ p.w.-$C^1$ and $f$ cts on $\gamma^*$ (finitely many bounded discontinuities allowed, by Lemma 2.2.1): $$\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\,\gamma'(t)\,dt,\qquad \ell(\gamma)=\int_a^b|\gamma'(t)|\,dt,\qquad \int_\gamma f\,|dz|=\int_a^b f(\gamma(t))\,|\gamma'(t)|\,dt.$$
  • Independent of parametrization (2.2.5, chain rule + substitution) — so it's an invariant of the oriented curve.
PropBasic properties + Estimation (ML) lemma (2.2.8, 2.2.10)
Hyp: $f,g$ cts on open $U$; $\gamma,\eta$ p.w.-$C^1$ in $U$.
  • Linearity; $\int_{\gamma^-}f=-\int_\gamma f$; $\int_{\gamma\star\eta}=\int_\gamma+\int_\eta$;
  • Estimation lemma: $\displaystyle\Big|\int_\gamma f\,dz\Big|\le \sup_{\gamma^*}|f|\cdot\ell(\gamma)$;
  • (2.2.10) $f_n\to f$ uniformly on $\gamma^*$ ⇒ $\int_\gamma f_n\to\int_\gamma f$ (immediate from est. lemma).
Sketch
  • Key input is the complex triangle inequality (2.2.3): write $\int f=re^{i\theta}$, project $f$ onto the $e^{i\theta}$ direction, use the real triangle inequality.
  • $\sup$ exists since $\gamma^*$ is compact.
  • This is the single most reused estimate in the course — Goursat, CIF's vanishing term, Liouville, residue calculus, Jordan's lemma all run on it.
ThmFTC for path integrals (2.2.13)
Hyp: $U$ open; $f:U\to\mathbb C$ cts with a primitive $F$ ($F'=f$ on $U$, Def 2.2.11); $\gamma:[a,b]\to U$ p.w.-$C^1$.
Then: $$\int_\gamma f(z)\,dz=F(\gamma(b))-F(\gamma(a)),$$ so $\oint_\gamma f=0$ around every closed path.
Sketch
  • $C^1$ case: $\frac{d}{dt}F(\gamma(t))=f(\gamma(t))\gamma'(t)$ by the path chain rule 2.2.12 (which uses the $\varepsilon$-form); apply real FTC to Re/Im.
  • p.w. case: telescope over the $C^1$ pieces.
Cor$f'\equiv0$ on a domain ⇒ constant; primitives unique up to constant (2.2.15, 2.2.17)
Hyp: $U$ a domain (connectedness essential!), $f'\equiv0$ on $U$.
Sketch
  • Domains are polygonally connected (Metric Spaces) ⇒ join $z_0\to w$ by a p.w.-$C^1$ path; $f(w)-f(z_0)=\int_\gamma f'=0$ by FTC.
ThmConverse FTC: vanishing loop integrals ⇒ primitive (2.2.16)
Hyp: $U$ a domain; $f$ cts (holo not needed!); $\oint_\gamma f=0$ for every closed path in $U$.
Then: $f$ has a primitive on $U$. Combined with 2.2.13: for cts $f$ on a domain, primitive exists $\iff$ all loop integrals vanish.
Sketch
  • Fix $z_0$; define $F(z)=\int_\gamma f$ along any path $z_0\to z$.
  • Well-defined: two paths concatenate to a loop, whose integral is $0$.
  • $F'=f$: append a straight segment $w\to z$ inside a ball; $\big|\frac{F(z)-F(w)}{z-w}-f(w)\big|\le\sup_t|f(w+t(z-w))-f(w)|\to0$ by continuity.
WarnThe fundamental counterexample: $1/z$ (2.2.18)
$$\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{dz}z=\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{ie^{it}}{e^{it}}dt=2\pi i\neq0 \;\Rightarrow\; 1/z \text{ has \textbf{no} primitive on } \mathbb C\setminus\{0\}.$$
  • Yet on $\mathbb C\setminus(-\infty,0]$ it does: $\operatorname{Log}$. Existence of a primitive depends on the domain, not just the function. This single computation drives winding numbers, CIF's "$f(w)\times$ orientation" term, and residues.

2.3  Cauchy's theorem

ThmCauchy / Cauchy–Goursat theorem (2.3.1)
Hyp:
  • $U$ a domain; $f$ holo on $U$ (differentiable only — no $C^1$ assumption on $f'$: that's Goursat's point);
  • $\gamma$ closed (not necessarily simple), with $\gamma^*$ and all bounded components of $\mathbb C\setminus\gamma^*$ inside $U$ ("no holes of $U$ inside $\gamma$").
Then: $\displaystyle\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz=0$.
Sketch — step 1: triangles (the bisection argument)
  • Subdivide $T$ into 4 similar triangles; new edges traversed twice with opposite orientations ⇒ cancel.
  • If $|I|=|\int_{\partial T}f|>0$, some quarter has $|\int|\ge|I|/4$; iterate: nested $T_n$ with $|\int_{\partial T_n}f|\ge 4^{-n}|I|$ and $\ell(\partial T_n)=2^{-n}\ell(\partial T)$.
  • Nested compacts shrink to a point $z_0$; differentiability there gives $f(z)=f(z_0)+f'(z_0)(z-z_0)+\epsilon(z)(z-z_0)$, $|\epsilon|\lt\varepsilon$ on $T_n$ for large $n$.
  • The linear part has a primitive ⇒ integrates to $0$ (FTC); the error gives $\big|\int_{\partial T_n}f\big|\le\varepsilon\,\ell(\partial T_n)^2 = \varepsilon\,4^{-n}\ell(\partial T)^2$ (est. lemma).
  • Combine: $|I|\le\varepsilon\,\ell(\partial T)^2$ for every $\varepsilon$ — contradiction. Holomorphy used only pointwise!
Sketch — step 2: star-like domains
  • $X$ convex: all segments $[z,w]\subseteq X$; star-like: some centre $z_0$ sees every point (Def 2.3.3; discs/triangles convex, the cross star-like not convex).
  • Define $F(z)=\int_{[z_0,z]}f$ along the canonical segment (no well-definedness issue).
  • For nearby $w,z$ the triangle $z_0,z,w$ lies in $U$ (star-likeness) ⇒ step 1 gives $\int_{\partial T}f=0$ ⇒ $F(z)-F(w)=\int_{[w,z]}f$; the 2.2.16 estimate then gives $F'=f$.
  • FTC ⇒ all loop integrals vanish on star-like $U$.
Sketch — step 3: general case non-exam outline
  • Closed polygons: decompose into simple polygons, triangulate, edges cancel.
  • General $\gamma$: Riemann sums $=$ integrals over inscribed polygonal curves ($=0$); justify the polygonal approximation limit.

2.4  Homotopy & the Deformation theorem

DefHomotopy; null-homotopic; simply connected (2.4.1, 2.4.2)
  • Paths $\gamma_0,\gamma_1$ from $a$ to $b$ in open $U$ are homotopic in $U$ if a cts $h:[0,1]^2\to U$ interpolates them with endpoints pinned.
  • For closed curves the definition differs: endpoints move freely but every intermediate curve $\gamma_s$ must stay closed. Null-homotopic = homotopic to a constant.
  • $U$ a domain is simply connected (s.c.) if any two paths with common endpoints are homotopic ⟺ every closed curve is null-homotopic (equivalence = nontrivial topology, quoted).
  • Star-like ⇒ s.c. (contract along the rays: $h(t,s)=sz_0+(1-s)\gamma(t)$, Ex 2.4.4). Annuli $A(z_0,r,R)$ are not s.c. (Ex 2.4.5). Quoted criterion: for bounded-ish $U$, s.c. $\iff \mathbb C\setminus U$ connected ("no holes").
ThmDeformation theorem (homotopy Cauchy) (2.4.7)
Hyp: $U$ a domain; $f$ holo on $U$; $\gamma_1,\gamma_2$ homotopic in $U$ (so: same endpoints, or both closed).
Then: $\displaystyle\int_{\gamma_1}f=\int_{\gamma_2}f$.
Sketch (proof = Thm A.0.2 in the appendix non-exam)
  • Compactness: $K=h([0,1]^2)$ admits $\varepsilon$ with $B(z,\varepsilon)\subseteq U$ for all $z\in K$; $h$ uniformly cts ⇒ chop the square into $N^2$ cells each mapping into an $\varepsilon$-disc.
  • Replace the level curves by piecewise-linear interpolants $\nu_k$ (the levels of a merely cts $h$ needn't be integrable! Rmk A.0.3).
  • On each disc $f$ has a primitive (convex ⇒ star-like Cauchy); adjacent primitives differ by constants; FTC + telescoping converts $\int_{\nu_k}$ into $\int_{\nu_{k+1}}$.
  • Moral: deformation = "locally, primitives always exist"; the theorem matters exactly on domains where no global primitive exists (Rmk 2.4.8).
  • Ex 2.4.12: in an annulus around $0$, any $\gamma$ homotopic to a counterclockwise circle has $\oint_\gamma dz/z=2\pi i$ — deformation with a nonzero common value.
ThmCauchy's theorem for s.c. domains (2.4.9)
Hyp: $U$ a s.c. domain, $f$ holo on $U$.
Then: path integrals depend only on endpoints; $\oint_\gamma f=0$ for every closed $\gamma$; hence (via 2.2.16) every holo $f$ on a s.c. domain has a primitive. (Converse also true — quoted, Rmk 2.4.10.)
  • Ex 2.4.11: $U$ s.c., $0\notin U$ ⇒ a holo branch of $\log$ exists on $U$ (a primitive of $1/z$).

2.5  Winding numbers

Prop+DefContinuous argument; winding number (2.5.1, 2.5.2)
  • 2.5.1 (lifting): any cts $\gamma:[0,1]\to\mathbb C\setminus\{0\}$ can be written $\gamma(t)=|\gamma(t)|e^{2\pi i a(t)}$ with $a$ cts, unique up to adding one integer globally.
  • 2.5.2: for closed $\gamma$ avoiding $z_0$: $\;I(\gamma,z_0)=a(1)-a(0)\in\mathbb Z$ (applied to $\gamma-z_0$) — the winding number / index. Defined for merely cts closed paths.
Sketch of 2.5.1
  • WLOG $|\gamma|=1$; uniform continuity ⇒ chop $[0,1]$ so each sub-arc stays in a half-plane, where a cts arg exists;
  • at the junctions the local args differ by integers — adjust and glue;
  • uniqueness: $a-b$ is cts, integer-valued, on connected $[0,1]$ ⇒ constant.
LemmaWinding number as an integral (2.5.4)
Hyp: $\gamma$ p.w.-$C^1$ closed, $z_0\notin\gamma^*$.
$$I(\gamma,z_0)=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\frac{dz}{z-z_0}.$$
Sketch
  • Write $\gamma=z_0+re^{2\pi ia}$ with $r,a$ p.w.-$C^1$; the integrand integrates to $[\log r(t)+2\pi i\,a(t)]_0^1=2\pi i(a(1)-a(0))$ since $r(0)=r(1)$.
  • Cut-plane payoff: if a simple curve $\Gamma$ joins $0$ to $\infty$, every loop in $\mathbb C\setminus\Gamma^*$ has $I=0$ ⇒ $\oint dz/z=0$ ⇒ (2.2.16) $1/z$ has a primitive there = a branch of log on any cut plane. Rmk 2.5.3: existence of a holo log-branch conversely forces $I=0$.
PropAnalyticity of Cauchy-type integrals — the M-test engine (2.5.6, 2.5.7)
Hyp: $U$ open, $\gamma$ a closed path in $U$, $f$ merely cts on $\gamma^*$ (no holomorphy!).
Then: $\;w\mapsto I_f(\gamma,w)=\dfrac1{2\pi i}\displaystyle\oint_\gamma\frac{f(z)}{z-w}\,dz\;$ is analytic on $\mathbb C\setminus\gamma^*$, with $$I_f^{(n)}(z_0)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz.$$ In particular ($f\equiv1$): $I(\gamma,\cdot)$ is cts and integer-valued ⇒ constant on each component of $\mathbb C\setminus\gamma^*$, and $=0$ on the unbounded one (est. lemma, Rmk 2.5.8).
Sketch (the user-requested one, verbatim move)
  • WLOG $z_0=0\notin\gamma^*$; pick $r$ with $B(0,2r)\cap\gamma^*=\emptyset$, so $|w/z|\lt\tfrac12$ for $w\in B(0,r)$, $z\in\gamma^*$.
  • Geometric series: $\dfrac{f(z)}{z-w}=\dfrac{f(z)}z\cdot\dfrac1{1-w/z}=\sum_{n\ge0}\dfrac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,w^n$.
  • $\gamma^*$ compact ⇒ $|f|\le M$ ⇒ terms dominated by $\frac M{2r}2^{-n}$ ⇒ Weierstrass M-test: uniform convergence in $z$ on $\gamma^*$.
  • Swap $\sum\leftrightarrow\oint$ (uniform convergence, 2.2.10): $I_f(\gamma,w)=\sum_n\big(\frac1{2\pi i}\oint\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}dz\big)w^n$ — a power series in $w$. Done.
DefPositively oriented; Jordan curve theorem (quoted) (2.5.9)
  • JCT (stated, not proved): a simple closed curve has exactly one bounded complementary component ("inside") and one unbounded; winding number on the inside is $\pm1$.
  • $\gamma$ positively oriented: $I(\gamma,w)=+1$ for $w$ inside (independent of $w$ by 2.5.6).
PropSimple curves are homotopic to small circles (2.5.10, ⚠ stated without proof)
Hyp: $U$ a domain; $\gamma$ simple, positively oriented, $\gamma\cup\text{inside}\subseteq U$; $w$ inside $\gamma$; $B(w,r)$ inside $\gamma$.
Then: $\gamma$ is homotopic to the circle $\gamma_r$ (about $w$) inside $U\setminus\{w\}$. This is the topological input that lets the Deformation theorem shrink contours in the CIF proof.

3.1–3.2  Cauchy's Integral Formula

ThmCauchy's Integral Formula (CIF) (3.1.1)
Hyp:
  • $U$ open (not nec. a domain/s.c.); $f$ holo on $U$;
  • $\gamma$ simple, positively oriented, closed, with $\gamma^*$ and its inside contained in $U$; $w$ inside $\gamma$.
Then: $$f(w)=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\frac{f(z)}{z-w}\,dz.$$ (Non-simple $\gamma$: weight the LHS by $I(\gamma,w)$ — Rmk 3.1.2.)
Sketch (deformation on shrinking circles + subtract-and-add)
  • $f(z)/(z-w)$ is holo on $U\setminus\{w\}$; by Prop 2.5.10 $\gamma\simeq\gamma_r$ there, so by the Deformation thm 2.4.7: $\oint_\gamma=\oint_{\gamma_r}$ for every small $r$.
  • Subtract and add $f(w)$: $$\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_r}\frac{f(z)}{z-w}dz=\underbrace{\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_r}\frac{f(z)-f(w)}{z-w}dz}_{\to\,0}\;+\;f(w)\cdot\underbrace{\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_r}\frac{dz}{z-w}}_{=\,I(\gamma_r,w)\,=\,1}.$$
  • Vanishing: $f$ differentiable at $w$ ⇒ the difference quotient is bounded near $w$; est. lemma with $\ell(\gamma_r)=2\pi r\to0$.
  • The LHS is $r$-independent (step 1) ⇒ it equals $f(w)$.
  • Honesty flag (Rmk 3.1.5): the Deformation thm is only proved in the appendix. For circles one can avoid it: split the annulus between two concentric circles into 4 star-like sectors where primitives exist; cuts cancel ⇒ the two circle integrals agree — and circles suffice for most applications.
CorCIF for multiple boundary curves (3.1.3)
Hyp: $U$ a bounded domain with p.w.-$C^1$ boundary in finitely many components; $f$ holo on (an open set containing) $\bar U$; outer boundary counterclockwise, inner boundaries clockwise.
Then: $\displaystyle\int_{\partial U}f=0$ and $\displaystyle\frac1{2\pi i}\int_{\partial U}\frac{f(z)}{z-w}dz=f(w)$ for $w\in U$.
Sketch
  • Add finitely many cuts joining the boundary components so $U$ minus the cuts is s.c.; apply CIF to the single resulting contour; each cut is traversed twice in opposite directions ⇒ cancels.
  • The two-circle case is the engine of Laurent's theorem.
ThmHomology form of Cauchy's theorem (3.2.5) non-exam
Hyp: $f$ holo on $U$; $\Gamma=\sum m_i\gamma_i$ a cycle in $U$ (formal $\mathbb Z$-combination of closed paths, Def 3.2.1) whose inside lies in $U$, i.e. $I(\Gamma,z)=0$ for all $z\notin U$.
Then: $\displaystyle\int_\Gamma f=0$, and for $z\in U\setminus\Gamma^*$: $\;\dfrac1{2\pi i}\displaystyle\int_\Gamma\dfrac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta-z}d\zeta=I(\Gamma,z)\,f(z)$.
Sketch (Dixon's argument — also Thm A.0.4)
  • Define $g_z(\zeta)=\frac{f(\zeta)-f(z)}{\zeta-z}$, extended by $g_z(z)=f'(z)$: holo in $\zeta$ (Riemann removable sing.), and $F(z)=\frac1{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma g_z$ is holo in $z$ (Prop 2.5.6 / Thm A.0.1).
  • Off the inside of $\Gamma$, $F$ agrees with $G(z)=\frac1{2\pi i}\int_\Gamma\frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta-z}d\zeta$ (there $I(\Gamma,z)=0$); glue $F$ on $U$ with $G$ on $V=\mathbb C\setminus(\Gamma^*\cup\mathrm{ins}\,\Gamma)$ ⇒ an entire $H$.
  • $H\to0$ at $\infty$ (est. lemma) ⇒ $H\equiv0$ by Liouville ⇒ integral formula; apply it to $(z-w)f(z)$ to get $\int_\Gamma f=0$.
  • Rmk 3.2.6 (dependency gold): this uses only consequences of Cauchy for a disc — the homology and homotopy forms are logically independent routes.

3.3  Applications: Taylor, Liouville, FTA, Morera

ThmTaylor's theorem: holo ⇒ analytic (Cor 3.3.2, Def 3.3.3)
Hyp: $U$ open, $f$ holo on $U$, $z_0\in U$, $B(z_0,R)\subseteq U$.
Then: $f$ equals its Taylor series on the whole of $B(z_0,R)$, and for any $r\lt R$: $$f^{(n)}(z_0)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-z_0|=r}\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz \tag{3.3.1}$$ ("Cauchy's Integral Formulae"). Hence holo ⟺ analytic ⟺ $C^\infty$ — the terms are used interchangeably from here on.
Sketch — two arrows meet here
  • From CIF (3.1.1): $f(w)=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_r}\frac{f(z)}{z-w}dz$ for $w$ inside the circle;
  • from Prop 2.5.6: the right-hand side is a Cauchy-type integral of a cts function, hence a power series in $w$ (geometric series + M-test + swap), with coefficients $\frac1{2\pi i}\oint\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}dz$;
  • power-series coefficients are derivatives (1.4.4/2.5.7) ⇒ formula (3.3.1).
  • Intuition (Rmk 3.3.1): $f$ inside = "convolution" of $f|_{\text{circle}}$ against the $C^\infty$ kernel $\frac1{z-w}$ — smoothness is inherited from the kernel.
ThmLiouville (3.3.4)
Hyp: $f$ entire (holo on all of $\mathbb C$, Def in §3.3) and bounded, $|f|\le M$.
Then: $f$ is constant.
Sketch
  • CIF on the circle $|z|=R$, two centres at once: $f(w)-f(0)=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_R}f(z)\big(\frac1{z-w}-\frac1z\big)dz=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_R}\frac{w\,f(z)}{z(z-w)}dz$.
  • Est. lemma: $|f(w)-f(0)|\le\frac{M|w|}{R-|w|}\to0$ as $R\to\infty$.
  • No real analogue: $1/(1+x^2)$ is bounded, real-analytic, non-constant (Rmk 3.3.5). The recurring dichotomy: entire functions are constant or unbounded — "as good as possible or as bad as possible".
ThmFundamental theorem of algebra (3.3.6)
Hyp: $p$ a non-constant polynomial over $\mathbb C$.
Then: $p$ has a root (hence, by induction, splits into linear factors — Rmk 3.3.7).
Sketch
  • If $p$ never vanishes, $1/p$ is entire.
  • Leading-term domination: $|p(z)|\ge\frac12|z|^n$ for $|z|\ge R$ ⇒ $1/p$ bounded (cts on the compact $\bar B(0,R)$, small outside).
  • Liouville ⇒ $1/p$ constant — contradiction. (Every proof of FTA hinges on the leading term dominating.)
ThmMorera (converse of Cauchy) (3.3.8, 3.3.9)
Hyp: $U$ open; $f$ cts on $U$; $\oint_\gamma f=0$ for every closed path in $U$.
Then: $f$ is holo.
Sketch
  • 2.2.16 ⇒ $f$ has a primitive $F$ (work on a ball/component if needed);
  • $F$ holo ⇒ $F$ is $C^\infty$ (Taylor 3.3.2!) ⇒ $f=F'$ holo.
  • Triangle version (3.3.9): holomorphy is local, and on a ball it's enough that $\int_{\partial T}f=0$ for all triangles $T$ with solid interior in $U$ — giving the characterisation: cts $f$ is holo $\iff$ all triangle integrals vanish.
PropLocally-uniform limits of holo are holo (3.3.10–3.3.12)
Hyp: $U$ a domain; holo $f_n\to f$ uniformly on compacts of $U$ (Def 3.3.10; e.g. power-series partial sums on $B(0,R)$ — uniform on every $\bar B(0,r)$, $r\lt R$, but usually not on $B(0,R)$, Ex 3.3.11).
Then: $f$ is holo.
Sketch
  • $f$ cts; on any ball $B(w,r)\subseteq U$ (convex ⇒ star-like Cauchy) $\oint_\gamma f_n=0$;
  • $\gamma^*$ compact ⇒ uniform convergence there ⇒ $\oint_\gamma f=\lim\oint_\gamma f_n=0$;
  • Morera on the ball.
  • Contrast with $\mathbb R$: uniform limits of real-differentiable functions can be nowhere differentiable. (Used later for Thm A.0.1: integrals depending on a parameter are holo.)

3.4  The identity theorem

PropZeros are isolated (or locally everything) (3.4.1, 3.4.2)
Hyp: $U$ open, $g$ holo on $U$, $g(z_0)=0$.
Then: either $g\equiv0$ near $z_0$, or $z_0$ is an isolated zero and $$g(z)=(z-z_0)^k g_1(z),\qquad g_1 \text{ holo},\; g_1(z_0)\neq0,$$ with $k\ge1$ unique — the multiplicity / order of vanishing.
Sketch
  • Taylor at $z_0$: all $c_n=0$ ⇒ $g\equiv0$ on the ball; else take $k=\min\{n:c_n\neq0\}$ and factor the series;
  • $g_1(z_0)=c_k\neq0$ + continuity ⇒ $g\neq0$ on a punctured ball;
  • uniqueness of $k$ by comparing growth as $z\to z_0$.
ThmIdentity theorem (3.4.3)
Hyp:
  • $U$ a domain — connectedness is the whole point;
  • $f_1,f_2$ holo on $U$; $S=\{f_1=f_2\}$ has a limit point inside $U$.
Then: $f_1\equiv f_2$ on all of $U$. (So agreement on any tiny disc, or on a convergent sequence with its limit in $U$, propagates globally — "analytic continuation is unique".)
Sketch (clopen/connectedness argument)
  • $g=f_1-f_2$; by 3.4.1 each zero is isolated or interior: $S=V\cup T$, $V=\operatorname{int}S$ open, $T$ = isolated zeros.
  • $S$ closed in $U$ (continuity), and limit points of $V$ can't be isolated ⇒ $V$ is open and closed in $U$.
  • $U$ connected ⇒ $V=\emptyset$ (then $S$ has no limit point — contradiction) or $V=U$.
  • The limit point must lie in $U$ (Rmk 3.4.4): $f(z)=e^{1/z}-1$ on $\mathbb C\setminus\{0\}$ vanishes at $1/(2\pi in)\to0$, but $0\notin U$ and $f\not\equiv0$.

3.5  Isolated singularities, Laurent series, residues

DefIsolated singularity; meromorphic; the trichotomy (3.5.1–3.5.3)
  • $z_0$ is an isolated singularity of $f$ if $f$ is holo on some $B(z_0,r)\setminus\{z_0\}$ but not at $z_0$.
  • Meromorphic on $U$: only isolated singularities, all of them poles.
  • Trichotomy:
    • removable: $f$ agrees near $z_0$ with a holo $g$ (e.g. $\frac{\sin z}z$ at $0$);
    • pole of order $n$: $f(z)=(z-z_0)^{-n}g(z)$, $g$ holo, $g(z_0)\neq0$ (e.g. $\frac{\sin z}{z^{n+1}}$);
    • essential: neither (e.g. $\sin(1/z)$, $e^{1/z}$).
ThmLaurent's theorem (3.5.9, Cor 3.5.14)
Hyp: $0\lt r\lt R$; $f$ holo on an open set containing the closed annulus $\bar A(z_0,r,R)$.
Then: on $A$, $$f(z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n(z-z_0)^n,\qquad c_n=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_s}\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz \;\;(\text{any } s\in[r,R]),$$ unique, converging uniformly on closed sub-annuli. Cor 3.5.14: at an isolated singularity, the expansion holds on the punctured disc $B(z_0,R)\setminus\{z_0\}$ (coefficients are $r$-independent; let $r\to0$). If $f$ is holo on the full ball, $c_{n\lt0}=0$ and Laurent = Taylor (3.5.12).
Sketch
  • Cor 3.1.3 on the annulus: $f(w)=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_R}\frac{f(z)}{z-w}dz-\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_r}\frac{f(z)}{z-w}dz$.
  • Outer: $|w|\lt|z|$ ⇒ $\frac1{z-w}=\sum_{n\ge0}\frac{w^n}{z^{n+1}}$ ⇒ nonnegative powers ("same way we showed holo ⇒ analytic", i.e. Prop 2.5.6's move).
  • Inner: $|z|\lt|w|$ ⇒ expand in $z/w$ ⇒ negative powers (mind the sign flip).
  • Uniform convergence ⇒ term-by-term integration ⇒ uniqueness of the $c_n$; the Deformation thm makes $c_n$ contour-independent (any curve homotopic to $\gamma_s$ in $A$ works, Rmk 3.5.11).
DefPrincipal part; residue (3.5.15–3.5.17)
  • Principal part $P_{z_0}f=\sum_{n\le-1}c_n(z-z_0)^n$ — converges on all of $\mathbb C\setminus\{z_0\}$, uniformly off any $B(z_0,r)$ (Prop 3.5.16).
  • Residue $\operatorname{Res}_{z_0}f=c_{-1}$. Why $c_{-1}$? Integrate term-by-term over a small circle: every $(z-z_0)^n$, $n\neq-1$, has a primitive ⇒ dies; only $\oint\frac{dz}{z-z_0}=2\pi i$ survives, giving $\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_{\gamma_s}f=c_{-1}$.
ThmLaurent classification of singularities (3.5.18)
At an isolated singularity:
  • removable $\iff$ no negative terms;
  • pole of order $n$ $\iff$ principal part is finite, lowest term $c_{-n}\neq0$;
  • essential $\iff$ infinitely many negative terms.
Sketch
  • No principal part ⇒ the series is a holo extension. Removable ⇒ negative coefficients are integrals of holo functions ⇒ vanish (Cauchy).
  • Finite principal part ⇒ $f=(z-z_0)^{-n}g$ with $g(z_0)=c_{-n}\neq0$ (factor the series); conversely Taylor-expand $g$.
  • Essential = elimination.
ThmRiemann's removable singularity theorem (3.5.21)
Hyp: $U$ open, $z_0\in U$, $f$ holo on $U\setminus\{z_0\}$.
Then: $z_0$ removable $\iff$ $f$ bounded near $z_0$.
Sketch ($h=(z-z_0)^2f$ trick)
  • Set $h=(z-z_0)^2f$, $h(z_0)=0$: the difference quotient at $z_0$ is $(z-z_0)f(z)\to0$ by boundedness ⇒ $h$ holo on all of $U$ with a double zero.
  • Taylor (3.3.2): $h=\sum_{k\ge2}a_k(z-z_0)^k$ ⇒ $f=\sum_{k\ge0}a_{k+2}(z-z_0)^k$ near $z_0$ — a holo extension.
  • Real contrast (Rmk 3.5.22): $\operatorname{sign}(x)$, $\sin(1/x)$, $|x|$ are bounded and analytic off $0$ but don't extend differentiably — impossible behaviours in $\mathbb C$.
LemmaPole $\iff$ $|f|\to\infty$ (3.5.23, 3.5.24)
$z_0$ is a pole $\iff |f(z)|\to\infty$ as $z\to z_0$; then $1/f$ (set $=0$ at $z_0$) is holo with a zero of order = order of the pole.
Sketch
  • (⇒) factor $f=(z-z_0)^{-n}g$.
  • (⇐) $1/f\to0$ ⇒ bounded ⇒ Riemann 3.5.21 extends it; its zero has finite order $n$ ⇒ $f=(z-z_0)^{-n}g^{-1}$.
  • On the Riemann sphere $\mathbb C_\infty$ removable and pole merge: $f$ extends $\mathbb C_\infty$-valued.
ThmCasorati–Weierstrass (3.5.25)
Hyp: $f$ holo on $U\setminus\{a\}$ with an essential singularity at $a$; $B(a,\rho)\subseteq U$.
Then: $f\big(B(a,\rho)\setminus\{a\}\big)$ is dense in $\mathbb C$. ("Either a limit exists, or it fails in the most spectacular way.")
Sketch
  • If some $z_0$ is missed by a margin, $g=1/(f-z_0)$ is bounded near $a$ ⇒ extends holomorphically (Riemann 3.5.21);
  • then $f=z_0+1/g$ has at most a pole at $a$ — contradiction.
  • Picard (quoted, Rmk 3.5.26): in fact $f$ takes every value infinitely often, with at most one exception — $e^{1/z}$ omits exactly $0$.
ThmResidue theorem (3.5.27)
Hyp:
  • $U$ open; $\gamma$ closed, with $\gamma^*$ and its inside in $U$;
  • $f$ holo on $U\setminus S$, $S$ finite, $S\cap\gamma^*=\emptyset$.
Then: $$\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz=\sum_{a\in S} I(\gamma,a)\operatorname{Res}_a(f).$$ (In practice $\gamma$ is simple and positively oriented, so the weights are $1$ inside, $0$ outside — Rmk 3.5.28.)
Sketch ("almost a trivial corollary by now")
  • Subtract all principal parts: $g=f-\sum_{a\in S}P_a(f)$ has only removable singularities ⇒ holo on $U$ ⇒ $\oint_\gamma g=0$ (Deformation thm: $\gamma$ is null-homotopic in $U$).
  • Each $P_a(f)$ converges uniformly on $\gamma^*$ (Prop 3.5.16) ⇒ integrate term-by-term;
  • powers $\neq-1$ have primitives ⇒ die; the $c_{-1}$ term gives $2\pi i\,I(\gamma,a)\operatorname{Res}_a f$ (Lemma 2.5.4).

3.6  Argument principle, Rouché, open mapping

LemmaLogarithmic derivative (3.6.1)
If $f$ has a zero (pole) of order $k$ at $z_0$, then $f'/f$ has a simple pole there with residue $k$ ($-k$): write $f=(z-z_0)^{\pm k}g$, $g(z_0)\neq0$, so $\frac{f'}f=\frac{\pm k}{z-z_0}+\frac{g'}g$ with $g'/g$ holo.
ThmArgument principle (3.6.2)
Hyp: $f$ meromorphic on $U$; $\gamma$ simple, positively oriented, $\gamma\cup\text{inside}\subseteq U$; no zeros/poles of $f$ on $\gamma^*$.
Then (zeros $N$, poles $P$ inside, counted with multiplicity): $$N-P=\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}\,dz=I(f\circ\gamma,\,0),$$ the number of times the image curve winds around $0$.
Sketch
  • Residue theorem applied to $f'/f$, whose residues are the orders $\pm k$ (Lemma 3.6.1);
  • last equality: substitute $w=f(\gamma(t))$ in $\oint_{f\circ\gamma}\frac{dw}w$.
ThmRouché (3.6.3)
Hyp: $f,g$ holo on open $U$; $\gamma$ simple, $\gamma\cup\text{interior}\subseteq U$; $\;|f|\gt|g|$ on $\gamma^*$.
Then: $f$ and $f+g$ have the same number of zeros inside $\gamma$ (with multiplicity). Slogan: count zeros of a hard function by comparing with an easy dominant one.
Sketch
  • Write $h=(f+g)/f$. Hyp ⇒ on $\gamma^*$ both $f\neq0$ and $f+g\neq0$ (since $|f+g|\ge|f|-|g|\gt0$), so the argument principle applies to each separately: $N_f=\tfrac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \tfrac{f'}{f}$, $\;N_{f+g}=\tfrac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \tfrac{(f+g)'}{f+g}$.
  • Quotient rule on $\gamma^*$: $\tfrac{h'}{h}=\tfrac{(f+g)'}{f+g}-\tfrac{f'}{f}$, so $N_{f+g}-N_f=\tfrac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \tfrac{h'}{h}$.
  • On $\gamma^*$, $|g/f|\lt1$ ⇒ $h\circ\gamma\subset B(1,1)\subset\{\operatorname{Re}\gt0\}$, where $\operatorname{Log}$ is a primitive of $1/w$ ⇒ $\oint_\gamma h'/h=0$. Hence $N_{f+g}=N_f$. $\;\square$
  • Don't argue "zeros of $h$ ↔ zeros of $f+g$, poles ↔ zeros of $f$, so #zeros=#poles": the converse fails at a common zero of $f,f+g$, where the orders cancel in $h$ (take $g=f/2$: $h\equiv\tfrac32$, no zeros/poles, yet $f,f+g$ share every zero). Counting $f$ and $f+g$ separately above sidesteps it — $h$ only ever supplies the difference of two clean holomorphic zero-counts.
  • Ex 3.6.5: $p(z)=z^4+5z+2$. On $|z|=2$: $|z^4|=16\gt12\ge|5z+2|$ ⇒ 4 roots in $B(0,2)$. On $|z|=1$: $|5z+2|\ge3\gt1=|z^4|$ ⇒ exactly 1 root in $B(0,1)$, hence 3 in the annulus.
ThmOpen mapping theorem (3.6.6, 3.6.7)
Hyp: $f$ holo and non-constant on a domain $U$ (domain, not just open — needs zeros of $f-w_0$ isolated, via the identity theorem).
Then: $f$ maps open sets to open sets. Refinement (degree, Rmk 3.6.7): if $f-w_0$ vanishes to order $d$ at $z_0$, then near $z_0$, $f$ is exactly $d$-to-$1$ onto a nbhd of $w_0$; $f'(z_0)\neq0$ ⇒ locally injective.
Sketch (Rouché perturbation)
  • $g=f-w_0$ has an isolated zero at $z_0$; pick $r$ with no other zeros in $\bar B(z_0,r)$ and $|g|\ge\delta$ on the boundary circle.
  • For $|w-w_0|\lt\delta$: $|w_0-w|\lt|g|$ on the circle ⇒ Rouché: $f-w$ has as many zeros as $f-w_0$ in $B(z_0,r)$, i.e. $\ge1$ ⇒ $B(w_0,\delta)\subseteq f(B(z_0,r))$.
ThmInverse function theorem (honest version) (3.6.8, 3.6.9)
Hyp: $f$ holo and injective on $U$ with $f'\neq0$ everywhere; $g=f^{-1}$ on $f(U)$.
Then: $g$ is holo with $g'(w)=1/f'(g(w))$.
Sketch
  • The hard part — continuity of $g$, which 1.4.12 had to assume — is now free: $g^{-1}(V)=f(V)$ is open by the open mapping theorem.
  • Then the difference-quotient computation of 1.4.12 finishes.
  • Bonus (Rmk 3.6.9 + Appendix B): $f'\neq0$ is automatic for injective holo $f$ — if $f'(z_0)=0$ then $f-f(z_0)=(z-z_0)^kg$ with $k\ge2$, and a holo branch of $g^{1/k}$ writes $f=w_0+\phi^k$ with $\phi'(z_0)\neq0$, so $f$ is locally $k$-to-1 (open mapping on $\phi$). Contrast $x\mapsto x^3$ on $\mathbb R$: "complex analysis is nicer".

3.7  Residue calculus toolbox

Computing residues

LemmaResidue at a pole of order $m$ (3.7.1)
$$\operatorname{Res}_{z_0}(f)=\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac1{(m-1)!}\,\frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}\Big[(z-z_0)^m f(z)\Big].$$ (Multiply away the pole; the $(m{-}1)$-th Taylor coefficient is $c_{-1}$.)
  • The workhorse (Rmk 3.7.2): for a simple pole of $f=g/h$ with $g(z_0)\neq0$, $h$ a simple zero: $$\operatorname{Res}_{z_0}\frac gh=\frac{g(z_0)}{h'(z_0)}.$$ E.g. $\operatorname{Res}_{\pi k}\frac{e^z}{\sin z}=\frac{e^{\pi k}}{\cos\pi k}$ (Ex 3.7.3).
  • Series manipulation often beats 3.7.1 (Ex 3.7.4–3.7.6): e.g. $\frac1{\sin^2z}=\frac1{z^2}+\frac13+\dots$ ⇒ $\operatorname{Res}_0=0$; and $\frac1{z^2\sinh^3z}=\frac1{z^5}-\frac1{2z^3}+\frac{17}{120\,z}+O(1)$ ⇒ $\operatorname{Res}_0=\frac{17}{120}$ — by geometric/binomial expansion of $\frac1{(1+g)^k}$ with $O(z^k)$ bookkeeping.

The contour catalogue

Tech1. Trig integrals → unit circle (3.7.7, 3.7.8)
  • $z=e^{it}$: $\cos t=\tfrac12(z+z^{-1})$, $\sin t=\tfrac1{2i}(z-z^{-1})$, $dt=\frac{dz}{iz}$ — any $\int_0^{2\pi}R(\cos t,\sin t)\,dt$ becomes a rational contour integral over $|z|=1$.
  • E.g. $\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{dt}{1+3\cos^2t}=\oint\frac{-4iz\,dz}{3+10z^2+3z^4}=2\pi i\big(\tfrac1{4i}+\tfrac1{4i}\big)=\pi$ (poles inside: $\pm i/\sqrt3$).
Tech2. Half-disk for $\int_{\mathbb R}$ of rational functions (3.7.9)
  • Close $[-R,R]$ with the upper semicircle; residues at UHP poles; est. lemma kills the arc when the integrand decays like $|z|^{-2}$ or faster.
  • E.g. $\int_0^\infty\frac{dx}{1+x^2+x^4}=\frac\pi{2\sqrt3}$: poles $e^{i\pi/3},e^{2i\pi/3}$ in the UHP, $\operatorname{Res}=1/p'$; arc $\le \pi R/(R^4-R^2-1)\to0$.
LemmaJordan's lemma — oscillatory integrands (3.7.10, 3.7.11, 3.7.12)
Hyp: $f$ cts on the upper semicircle $\gamma_R$; $\alpha\gt0$.
$$\Big|\int_{\gamma_R}f(z)e^{i\alpha z}\,dz\Big|\le\frac\pi\alpha\,M_R,\qquad M_R=\max_{\gamma_R^*}|f|;$$ so if $f\to0$ at $\infty$ in the UHP (holo off finitely many singularities), the arc integral $\to0$ — even though the arc length grows like $R$.
Sketch
  • Concavity of $\sin$ (Lemma 3.7.10, via MVT twice): $\sin t\ge\frac{2t}\pi$ on $[0,\frac\pi2]$;
  • $|e^{i\alpha z}|=e^{-\alpha R\sin t}\le e^{-2\alpha Rt/\pi}$ ⇒ $\big|\int\big|\le 2RM_R\int_0^{\pi/2}e^{-2\alpha Rt/\pi}dt\lt\frac\pi\alpha M_R$.
  • Variants (3.7.12): sub-arcs/sectors, same proof; $\alpha\lt0$ ⇒ use the lower half-plane (apply to $f(-z)$).
  • Standard trick: for $\int\frac{\sin x}x$-type integrals use $e^{iz}$, never $\sin z$ ($\sin$ blows up in the UHP); take Im at the end.
LemmaIndentation lemma — simple pole on the contour (3.7.15; 3.7.13–3.7.14)
Hyp: $f$ meromorphic with a simple pole at $a$; $\gamma_\varepsilon(t)=a+\varepsilon e^{it}$, $t\in[\alpha,\beta]$.
$$\lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\int_{\gamma_\varepsilon}f(z)\,dz=i\,(\beta-\alpha)\operatorname{Res}_a(f)$$ — a half-turn picks up $\pm\pi i\cdot\operatorname{Res}$ (sign = direction).
Sketch
  • $f=\frac c{z-a}+g$, $g$ holo ⇒ bounded ⇒ its bit is $O(\varepsilon)$ (est. lemma); the $\frac c{z-a}$ term computes exactly to $ic(\beta-\alpha)$.
  • Worked classic, both ways (3.7.13/3.7.14): $\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{\sin x}x\,dx=\pi$. Either integrate the entire function $\frac{e^{iz}-1}z$ (removable singularity — no indent needed; Jordan kills $e^{iz}/z$ on the arc, $\oint\frac{dz}z$ over the arc $=i\pi$); or indent at $0$ and collect $-i\pi\cdot\operatorname{Res}_0\frac{e^{iz}}z=-i\pi$ from the clockwise half-circle.
Tech3. Summation of series: squares + $\cot\pi z$ (3.7.16–3.7.19)
  • $\cot\pi z$ has simple poles at every $n\in\mathbb Z$, each with residue $\frac1\pi$ (Laurent: $\cot z=\frac1z-\frac z3+O(z^3)$).
  • Lemma 3.7.16: on the squares $\Gamma_N$ with vertices $(N{+}\tfrac12)(\pm1\pm i)$, $|\cot\pi z|\le C$ uniformly in $N$ (horizontal sides via $\coth$, vertical via $\tanh$, periodicity).
  • For $\sum\frac1{n^2}$: integrate $g=\frac{\cot\pi z}{z^2}$ over $\Gamma_N$; est. lemma: $\big|\oint g\big|\le C\cdot\frac{\text{perimeter}}{N^2}\to0$; residue thm: $0=\operatorname{Res}_0+\sum_{n\neq0}\frac1{\pi n^2}$ with $\operatorname{Res}_0=-\frac\pi3$ ⇒ $\boxed{\sum_{n\ge1}\frac1{n^2}=\frac{\pi^2}6}$.
  • Same scheme: $\sum\frac1{n^2+1}$ (extra residues at $\pm i$), $\zeta(2k)$ from deeper Laurent coefficients of $\cot$ non-exam; $\zeta$ continues to $\mathbb C\setminus\{1\}$ uniquely by the identity theorem.
Tech4. Keyhole contours: multivalued integrands (3.7.20)
  • For $\int_0^\infty\frac{x^{1/2}}{1+x^2}dx$: take the branch $z^{1/2}=r^{1/2}e^{it/2}$, $t\in[0,2\pi)$ (cut on $\mathbb R_{\gt0}$), and the keyhole $\Gamma_{R,\varepsilon}$ hugging the cut.
  • ML estimates kill both circles ($R^{1/2}/R^2\to0$; $\varepsilon^{1/2}\to0$).
  • Key point: across the cut $z^{1/2}$ flips sign, and the lower segment is traversed backwards — the two straight pieces add instead of cancelling: total $=2\int_0^\infty$.
  • Residues at $\pm i$ computed in the chosen branch ($i=e^{i\pi/2}$, $-i=e^{3i\pi/2}$): $2\pi i(\tfrac12e^{-i\pi/4}+\tfrac12e^{-3i\pi/4})=\pi\sqrt2$ ⇒ integral $=\frac\pi{\sqrt2}$.

ThmParameter integrals are holo (A.0.1)
Hyp: $U$ open; $F:U\times[a,b]\to\mathbb C$ with $z\mapsto F(z,s)$ holo for each $s$, and $F$ cts jointly.
Then: $f(z)=\int_a^b F(z,s)\,ds$ is holo on $U$.
Sketch
  • Riemann sums $f_n(z)=\frac1n\sum_i F(z,i/n)$ are holo (finite sums);
  • uniform continuity of $F$ on $K\times[0,1]$ ⇒ $f_n\to f$ uniformly on compacts ⇒ Prop 3.3.12.
ThmProof of the Deformation theorem (A.0.2, A.0.3)
  • The grid-of-discs argument sketched at Thm 2.4.7 above; the subtlety A.0.3: interpolate with piecewise-linear paths because the homotopy's level curves are only cts, hence possibly non-integrable.
ThmHomology/winding-number Cauchy for paths (A.0.4, A.0.5)
Hyp: $f$ holo on $U$; $\gamma$ closed in $U$ with $I(\gamma,z)=0$ for all $z\notin U$ ("inside ⊆ $U$").
Then: $\oint_\gamma f=0$ and $\frac1{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma\frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta-z}d\zeta=I(\gamma,z)f(z)$. If $U$ is s.c. this applies to every closed path in $U$ (then $1/(z-w)$ is holo on $U$ for $w\notin U$, so $I(\gamma,w)=0$ by homotopy Cauchy).
  • Same Dixon glue-an-entire-function proof as 3.2.5. A.0.5: in practice it's usually easier to verify "$U$ s.c." than to compute a path's inside; and the bookkeeping shows homology and homotopy forms are established essentially independently.
ClaimAppendix B: injectivity forces $f'\neq0$
If $f$ holo, non-constant, $f'(z_0)=0$, then $f$ is at least 2-to-1 near $z_0$.
Sketch
  • $f-w_0=(z-z_0)^kh$, $h(z_0)\neq0$, $k\ge2$; a holo $k$-th-root branch on a slit disc gives $\phi=(z-z_0)h^{1/k}$ with $f=w_0+\phi^k$, $\phi'(z_0)\neq0$;
  • open mapping: $\phi$ covers a ball $B(0,r)$, and $z\mapsto z^k$ is exactly $k$-to-1 there.
