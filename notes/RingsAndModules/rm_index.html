0  The big picture

One foundation, two big arcs. Foundations: rings → ideals → quotients, with the dictionary $R/I$ is a domain $\iff I$ prime, a field $\iff I$ maximal. Arc 1 — the factorisation tower: $\textbf{ED}\Rightarrow\textbf{PID}\Rightarrow\textbf{UFD}$, with $\mathbb Z$ and $k[t]$ the running examples, feeding field theory. Arc 2 — the structure theorem: Smith normal form → present a module by a relation matrix → structure theorem → rational & Jordan canonical forms (via "a $k[t]$-module = a vector space with an operator"). Red = the load-bearing chains.

1  Rings, subrings, homomorphisms, $R[t]$

DefRing; subring; homomorphism; characteristic (1.1–1.8)
  • Ring $(R,+,\times,0,1)$: $(R,+)$ abelian group, $\times$ associative with identity $1$, distributive. Convention: rings have a 1; the zero ring ($1=0$) is allowed; commutative from the end of §1 on.
  • Subring (1.3): contains $0,1$, closed under $+,\times$. Criterion (1.4): $S\subseteq R$ is a subring $\iff 1\in S$ and $s_1-s_2, s_1s_2\in S$ (subgroup test + closure).
  • Homomorphism (1.5): $f(1)=1$, additive, multiplicative. (Because $f(1)=1$ is required, kernels are never subrings — they're ideals.) Iso = bijective hom.
  • Characteristic (1.8): the $d\ge0$ with $\{n\cdot 1_R\}\cong\mathbb Z/d\mathbb Z$ (image of the unique hom $\mathbb Z\to R$, the smallest subring).
ExThe example zoo (1.2, 1.7)
  • $\mathbb Z$, $\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z$, fields $\mathbb Q,\mathbb R,\mathbb C$; $\mathbb Z[i]$ (Gaussian integers, "$\mathbb Z$-like" — future PID); $M_n(k)$ and $\mathrm{End}(V)$ (noncommutative); $R\oplus S$, $R^X$.
  • Polynomials are not functions: over $\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z$ there are only finitely many functions $R\to R$ but $R[t]$ is infinite.
  • $\mathbb C\cong\left\{\begin{pmatrix}a&-b\\b&a\end{pmatrix}:a,b\in\mathbb R\right\}\subseteq M_2(\mathbb R)$ (1.7iv).
Lemma$R[t]$ via convolution; evaluation homomorphisms (§1.1, 1.10)
$R[t]$ is built rigorously as finitely-supported sequences $\mathbb N\to R$ with convolution $(f*g)(n)=\sum_{k+l=n}f(k)g(l)$ (a subring of the power series ring $R[[t]]$). Lemma 1.10: a hom $\Phi:R[t]\to S$ is determined by, and freely specified by, the pair $(\phi=\Phi|_R,\ s=\Phi(t))$ — "say where the coefficients go and where $t$ goes."
Sketch
  • $\Phi(\sum a_it^i)=\sum\phi(a_i)s^i$ is forced (uniqueness) and is checked to be a hom (existence).

2  Domains, units, field of fractions

DefIntegral domain; units (2.1, 2.5)
  • ID: nonzero ring with no zero-divisors ($ab=0\Rightarrow a=0$ or $b=0$) $\iff$ cancellation holds.
  • Units $R^\times$ form a group: $\mathbb Z^\times=\{\pm1\}$, $k^\times=k\setminus\{0\}$, $M_n(k)^\times=GL_n(k)$.
  • 2.3/2.4: a subring of an ID is an ID; $\operatorname{char}$ of an ID (hence a field) is $0$ or prime. A finite ID is a field (2.7).
PropField of fractions + its universal property (2.8, 2.9)
Hyp: $R$ an integral domain (cancellation is exactly what the construction needs).
Then: $F(R)=\{a/b: b\neq0\}/\!\sim$ (with $a/b\sim c/d\iff ad=bc$) is a field, $R\hookrightarrow F(R)$. Universal property (2.9): any embedding $\theta:R\hookrightarrow k$ into a field extends uniquely to $\tilde\theta:F(R)\hookrightarrow k$ — so $F(R)$ is the smallest field containing $R$ ($\mathbb Q$ from $\mathbb Z$).
Sketch
  • $\sim$ is an equivalence relation (transitivity uses cancellation — why an ID is needed); operations well-defined; $a/b\neq0\Rightarrow b/a$ inverse.
  • Universal property: $\tilde\theta(a/b)=\theta(a)\theta(b)^{-1}$ is forced, well-defined, a hom.
  • 2.10: every characteristic-$0$ field contains a unique copy of $\mathbb Q$.

3  Ideals, quotients, the isomorphism theorems

DefIdeal; generated/principal ideals; associates (3.2–3.11)
  • Ideal $I\lhd R$: additive subgroup with $a\in I, r\in R\Rightarrow ar\in I$ ("absorbs" multiplication). $1\in I\Rightarrow I=R$.
  • $\langle T\rangle=R\cdot T$ = smallest ideal containing $T$ (3.6/3.7, the "span" analogue); principal = $\langle a\rangle=aR$.
  • Associates: $a=ub$, $u\in R^\times$. In a domain, generators of a principal ideal are exactly the associates (3.11).
ThmQuotient ring; ideals = kernels; universal property (3.12–3.15)
For $I\lhd R$, $R/I$ is a ring and $q:R\to R/I$ a surjective hom with $\ker q=I$ (3.12). Hence ideals are exactly the kernels (3.13). Universal property (3.14): a hom $\phi:R\to S$ with $I\subseteq\ker\phi$ factors uniquely through $q$, with $\ker\bar\phi=\ker\phi/I$.
Sketch
  • $\times$ on cosets is well-defined precisely because $I$ absorbs multiplication: $(r_1+i_1)(r_2+i_2)=r_1r_2+(\text{stuff in }I)$.
  • Universal property: surjectivity of $q$ forces $\bar\phi(r+I)=\phi(r)$; well-defined since $I\subseteq\ker\phi$.
CorThe three isomorphism theorems (3.16)
  • First: $R/\ker\phi\cong\operatorname{im}\phi$.
  • Second: $A$ subring, $I$ ideal $\Rightarrow (A+I)/I\cong A/(A\cap I)$.
  • Third: $I_1\subseteq I_2$ ideals $\Rightarrow (R/I_1)/(I_2/I_1)\cong R/I_2$.
Sketch
  • All three are the universal property (3.14) + first iso theorem applied to the right map — the same template re-used for modules in §8.
  • Ex 3.17: for $\alpha\in\mathrm{End}(V)$, $k[t]\to\mathrm{End}(V)$ has image $\cong k[t]/\langle m_\alpha\rangle$ — the minimal polynomial generates the (principal) kernel. This is the seed of §12.
ThmChinese Remainder Theorem (3.18, 3.19)
Hyp: ideals $I,J$ with $I+J=R$ ("comaximal").
Then: $R/(I\cap J)\cong R/I\oplus R/J$. Classically: $m,n$ coprime $\Rightarrow \mathbb Z/mn\cong\mathbb Z/m\oplus\mathbb Z/n$.
Sketch
  • $r\mapsto(r+I,r+J)$ has kernel $I\cap J$; surjective because $I+J=R$ lets you hit any pair of residues. First iso theorem.
  • This is exactly the engine of the primary decomposition form of the structure theorem (Lemma 11.8).
PropIdeal correspondence (3.20–3.22)
For a surjection $\phi:R\to S$ with $K=\ker\phi$: $I\mapsto\phi(I)$ and $J\mapsto\phi^{-1}(J)$ are mutually inverse bijections between $\{$ideals of $S\}$ and $\{$ideals of $R$ containing $K\}$. (For $q:R\to R/I$: ideals of $R/I$ ↔ ideals of $R$ containing $I$.)
  • This is what makes "$R/I$ field $\iff$ $I$ maximal" work — maximality of $I$ = $R/I$ has no nontrivial ideals.

4  Prime/maximal ideals; Euclidean domains & PIDs

LemmaThe quotient dictionary (4.1, 4.2)
For an ideal $I\lhd R$ ($R$ commutative):
  • $I$ prime ($ab\in I\Rightarrow a\in I$ or $b\in I$, $I\neq R$) $\iff$ $R/I$ is an integral domain;
  • $I$ maximal (no proper ideal strictly contains it) $\iff$ $R/I$ is a field;
  • hence maximal $\Rightarrow$ prime (fields are domains).
Sketch
  • $(a+I)(b+I)=0\iff ab\in I$ — directly gives the prime⟺domain equivalence.
  • Field ⟺ no nontrivial ideals; apply the ideal correspondence (3.22) between ideals of $R/I$ and ideals of $R$ containing $I$.
  • $\mathbb Z$ (Ex 4.4): ideals are $d\mathbb Z$; prime $\iff d$ prime; nonzero primes are maximal.
LemmaDivision algorithm ⟹ $k[t]$ is a PID (4.6, 4.7)
Hyp: 4.6: $R$ any ring, $f\in R[t]$ with unit leading coefficient. 4.7: $k$ a field.
4.6: unique $q,r$ with $g=qf+r$, $\deg r\lt\deg f$. 4.7: every nonzero ideal of $k[t]$ is $\langle f\rangle$ for a unique monic $f$ — so $k[t]$ is a PID.
Sketch
  • 4.6: induct on $\deg g$, subtracting $a_n^{-1}b_m t^{m-n}f$ to kill the leading term (needs the leading coeff invertible).
  • 4.7: take $f\in I$ of minimal degree; division forces every $g\in I$ to have remainder $0$ — the "least-degree element generates" pattern.
  • $k[t]/\langle f\rangle$ has $k$-basis $\{1,t,\dots,t^{\deg f-1\,}\}$ — "adjoin a root of $f$." E.g. $\mathbb C=\mathbb R[t]/\langle t^2+1\rangle$ (Ex 4.8).
Def+LemEuclidean domain ⟹ PID (4.10–4.14)
Def 4.10: an ID with $N:R\setminus\{0\}\to\mathbb N$ such that any $a,b$ ($b\neq0$) give $a=bq+r$ with $r=0$ or $N(r)\lt N(b)$. Examples: $\mathbb Z$ ($N=|\cdot|$), $k[t]$ ($N=\deg$), $\mathbb Z[i]$ ($N=a^2+b^2$, Lemma 4.12).
Lemma 4.13: every ideal of a Euclidean domain is principal — ED ⟹ PID.
Sketch (the "least norm" argument)
  • In a nonzero ideal $I$ pick $d$ of minimal norm $N(d)$; for any $m\in I$, $m=qd+r$ forces $r=m-qd\in I$ with $N(r)\lt N(d)$ — impossible unless $r=0$. So $I=Rd$.
  • $\mathbb Z[i]$ Euclidean (4.12): round $s/t\in\mathbb C$ to the nearest Gaussian integer; the error has $N\lt 1$, so $N(s-qt)\lt N(t)$.
LemmaIn a PID: irreducible ⟺ prime ⟺ maximal (4.16, 4.17)
Hyp: $R$ a PID, $d\neq0$. (Irreducible: non-unit, $d=ab\Rightarrow a$ or $b$ a unit. Prime: $d\mid ab\Rightarrow d\mid a$ or $d\mid b$.)
TFAE: (1) $\langle d\rangle$ prime; (2) $d$ irreducible; (3) $\langle d\rangle$ maximal.
Sketch
  • (1)⇒(2): $d=ab\in\langle d\rangle$ prime ⇒ (say) $a\in\langle d\rangle$, $a=rd$; cancel in the domain ⇒ $b$ a unit.
  • (2)⇒(3): $\langle d\rangle\subseteq\langle a\rangle$ ⇒ $d=ab$ ⇒ irreducibility makes $a$ a unit ($\langle a\rangle=R$) or $b$ a unit ($\langle a\rangle=\langle d\rangle$).
  • (3)⇒(1): maximal ⇒ prime always.
  • In any ID: prime ⇒ irreducible; the converse needs a PID. (Failure of the converse is exactly the non-UFD phenomenon — see $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-17}]$ below.)

5  Fields, the tower law, minimal polynomials

Def+CorField extensions; the tower law (5.2–5.5)
$E/F$ a field extension; degree $[E:F]=\dim_F E$. Tower law (5.5): $F\subseteq E\subseteq K$ ⇒ $[K:F]=[K:E]\,[E:F]$ (finite iff both factors are).
Sketch
  • If $\{x_j\}$ is an $F$-basis of $E$ and $\{e_i\}$ an $E$-basis of $K$, then $\{x_je_i\}$ is an $F$-basis of $K$ (5.3) — count $=[E:F][K:E]$.
  • Finite fields (5.1): a finite field has order $p^d$ (it's a $d$-dim space over its prime subfield $\mathbb F_p$).
LemmaMinimal polynomial (5.6–5.8)
Hyp: $E/F$ finite, $\alpha\in E$ (subfields of $\mathbb C$).
Then: there's a unique monic irreducible $f\in F[t]$ with $F(\alpha)\cong F[t]/\langle f\rangle$ (the minimal polynomial), and $[F(\alpha):F]=\deg f$.
Sketch — where the whole tower pays off
  • $\{1,\alpha,\dots,\alpha^d\}$ ($d=[F(\alpha):F]$) is dependent ⇒ $\ker(F[t]\xrightarrow{t\mapsto\alpha}E)\neq0$;
  • $F[t]$ a PID ⇒ kernel $=\langle f\rangle$; the image is a domain ⇒ $\langle f\rangle$ prime ⇒ (Lemma 4.17) maximal ⇒ image is a field, namely $F(\alpha)$.
  • Ex 5.10–5.11: $\mathbb Q(\sqrt3)\cong\mathbb Q[t]/\langle t^2-3\rangle$; $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt3,2^{1/3}):\mathbb Q]=6$ by the tower law (lcm of $2,3$, then check $\sqrt3\notin\mathbb Q(2^{1/3})$).

6  Unique factorisation: the ED ⊊ PID ⊊ UFD tower

DefUFD; the equivalences (6.4, 6.5)
UFD: an ID where every nonzero non-unit factors into irreducibles, uniquely up to order and units. Lemma 6.5, TFAE: (1) $R$ a UFD; (2) every irreducible is prime and factorisations exist; (3) every nonzero non-unit is a product of primes.
Sketch
  • Uniqueness comes for free once "irreducible = prime": a prime in one factorisation must match (up to a unit) a factor of the other (induction on the number of factors).
ThmPID ⟹ UFD (6.6, 6.8)
Hyp: $R$ a PID.
Then: $R$ is a UFD.
Sketch (existence + uniqueness split)
  • Uniqueness is free: in a PID irreducible ⟺ prime (Lemma 4.17), so Lemma 6.5 applies.
  • Existence via the ascending chain condition (6.6): a PID is Noetherian — any chain $I_1\subseteq I_2\subseteq\cdots$ stabilises (their union is principal, $=\langle c\rangle$, and $c$ appears at some finite stage). A non-terminating factorisation would give a strictly ascending chain $\langle a_1\rangle\subsetneq\langle a_2\rangle\subsetneq\cdots$ — contradiction.
ThmGauss's lemma ⟹ $\mathbb Z[t]$, $R[t]$ are UFDs (6.11–6.19)
Content $c(f)$ = hcf of coefficients. Gauss (6.12): $c(fg)=c(f)c(g)$. Consequence: $\mathbb Z[t]$ is a UFD (6.16), and more generally $R$ a UFD ⟹ $R[t]$ a UFD (6.19) (so $\mathbb Z[t]$, $\mathbb Q[x,y]$ are UFDs).
Sketch
  • Gauss: reduce mod a prime $p$; $\mathbb F_p[t]$ is a domain, so $p\mid c(fg)\iff p\mid c(f)$ or $p\mid c(g)$.
  • $\mathbb Z[t]$: factor over the PID $\mathbb Q[t]$, then use content (6.13/6.15) to pull the factorisation back into $\mathbb Z[t]$; primes of $\mathbb Z[t]$ = primes of $\mathbb Z$ + content-1 irreducibles of $\mathbb Q[t]$.
  • Eisenstein (6.21): if a prime $p$ divides all coeffs but the top, and $p^2\nmid a_0$, then $f$ is irreducible — e.g. $t^3-2$, so $2^{1/3}\notin\mathbb Q$.

7  Modules: definition & examples

DefModule; the two key bridges (7.1, 7.4, 7.6)
An $R$-module = abelian group $M$ + action $R\times M\to M$ satisfying $1m=m$, $(rs)m=r(sm)$, and both distributive laws. "Linear algebra over a ring."
  • Bridge 1 (Lemma 7.4): an $R$-module structure on $M$ = a ring hom $R\to\operatorname{End}(M)$.
  • Examples (7.6): $R$ field ⇒ vector space; abelian groups = $\mathbb Z$-modules (uniquely); $R$, ideals $I$, and $R/I$ are modules; free module $R^n$.
  • Bridge 2 (7.6.8) — the one that powers §12: a $k[t]$-module = a $k$-vector space $V$ together with a chosen operator ($t$ acts as some $T\in\operatorname{End}_k V$). The hom $k[t]\to\operatorname{End}(V)$ is "send $t\mapsto T$." So a $k[t]$-module is a pair $(V,T)$.
DefSubmodules, generation, linear independence, free, f.g. (7.7–7.9)
  • Submodule = subgroup closed under the action; $\langle X\rangle=R\cdot X$. A subset of $R$ is a submodule $\iff$ it's an ideal.
  • Linearly independent / basis / free (has a basis) / finitely generated — same words as for vector spaces, but the behaviour is very different (below).

8  Quotient modules & the isomorphism theorems

Def+CorQuotient modules; the three iso theorems (8.1–8.8)
For a submodule $N\le M$, the quotient $M/N$ is a module; module homs have kernels/images that are submodules. The universal property (8.7) and the three isomorphism theorems (8.8) hold, with proofs identical to the ring case (§3) — "word for word."
  • $R$ field ⇒ these are the vector-space iso theorems; $R=\mathbb Z$ ⇒ the abelian-group ones.
  • Submodule correspondence (8.5): submodules of $M/N$ ↔ submodules of $M$ containing $N$.

9  Free, torsion, torsion-free; Hom = matrix

DefAnnihilator, torsion, torsion-free (9.1–9.5)
Standing assumption from §9: $R$ an integral domain.
  • $\operatorname{Ann}(m)=\{r:rm=0\}$ (an ideal); $m$ torsion if $\operatorname{Ann}(m)\neq0$. $M$ torsion / torsion-free accordingly.
  • Cyclic module $Rm\cong R/\operatorname{Ann}(m)$ (9.2). $R$ is torsion-free over itself $\iff$ ID.
  • 9.5: $M^{\mathrm{tor}}$ is a submodule and $M/M^{\mathrm{tor}}$ is torsion-free (uses ID: $s,t\neq0\Rightarrow st\neq0$).
WarnModules are not vector spaces (9.4, §10 intro)
  • A finite abelian group is a $\mathbb Z$-module that is not free (a free module is infinite) and is all torsion.
  • Free ⊊ torsion-free in general: $\langle x,y\rangle\lhd\mathbb C[x,y]$ is torsion-free but not free (9.4). (Over a PID, f.g. torsion-free will be free — Cor 11.6.)
  • Steinitz exchange fails: $\{2\}$ is a basis of the submodule $2\mathbb Z\le\mathbb Z$ but can't be extended to a basis of $\mathbb Z$ (whose only bases are $\{\pm1\}$). Smith normal form quantifies exactly this failure.
Lem+PropRank; submodules of free modules over a PID are free (9.6, 9.7)
9.6: over any ring, a f.g. free module has a well-defined rank (reduce mod a maximal ideal $I$: $\dim_{R/I}(M/IM)$ is basis-independent). 9.7: over a PID, every submodule of $R^n$ is free of rank $\le n$.
Sketch of 9.7 (induction on $n$)
  • $n=1$: submodules of $R$ are ideals; PID ⇒ principal ⇒ free of rank $\le1$.
  • $n\gt1$: intersect with the rank-$(n-1)$ submodule $W$; $N\cap W$ free by induction; $N/(N\cap W)\hookrightarrow M/W\cong R$ is $0$ or rank $1$; assemble a basis.
  • Converse (9.8): over an ID, "every submodule of every free module is free" $\iff$ $R$ is a PID. This is the property §11 leans on.
CorHomomorphisms of free modules = matrices, up to $A\sim PAQ$ (9.12–9.17)
Choosing bases, a hom $R^m\to R^n$ ↔ a matrix $A\in\operatorname{Mat}_{n,m}(R)$; composition = matrix product; isomorphisms ↔ $GL_n(R)$ (matrices with $\det\in R^\times$). Changing bases replaces $A$ by $PAQ$ with $P\in GL_n(R)$, $Q\in GL_m(R)$.
  • So a hom of free modules ↔ an equivalence class of matrices under $A\sim PAQ$. A "canonical form" = a canonical representative of that class — supplied next by Smith normal form.

10  Smith normal form

ThmSmith normal form (10.1–10.3)
Hyp: $A\in\operatorname{Mat}_{n,m}(R)$ over a Euclidean domain $R$. (Elementary row/column ops = left/right multiplication by invertible matrices.)
Then: $A$ is equivalent (by elementary row & column operations) to a diagonal $\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_k)$ (padded with zeros), with the divisibility chain $d_1\mid d_2\mid\cdots\mid d_k$. The $d_i$ are unique up to units.
Sketch (least-norm pivot + gcd)
  • Bring an entry of minimal norm to the $(1,1)$ slot. If it doesn't divide some entry in its row/column, Euclidean division produces a strictly smaller-norm remainder — recurse (norm can't drop forever).
  • Once $a_{11}$ divides its whole row and column, clear them; if a deeper entry isn't divisible by $a_{11}$, add its row into row 1 and repeat. End with $a_{11}\mid$ everything; factor it out and induct on the smaller block — forcing $d_1\mid d_2\mid\cdots$.

11  The structure theorem

Prop+DefPresentations: a module as a relation matrix (11.1–11.3)
Hyp: $M$ a f.g. module over a PID.
There is a resolution $0\to R^m\xrightarrow{\psi}R^n\xrightarrow{\phi}M\to0$: generators give the surjection $\phi$; $\ker\phi$ is free (Prop 9.7, over a PID) giving the injection $\psi$. So $M\cong R^n/\operatorname{im}\psi$, and the matrix of $\psi$ is the relation matrix — it records all $R$-linear dependencies among the generators. Up to iso, $M$ is captured by that matrix.
ThmStructure theorem — invariant factor form (11.4, 11.5)
Hyp: $M$ a finitely generated module over a Euclidean domain (true for PIDs too).
Then: $$M\;\cong\;\big(R/c_1R\big)\oplus\cdots\oplus\big(R/c_rR\big)\;\oplus\;R^s,\qquad c_1\mid c_2\mid\cdots\mid c_r,$$ with the $c_i$ nonzero non-units, unique up to units, and $s$ unique.
Sketch — Smith normal form does all the work
  • Take a presentation $R^m\xrightarrow{\psi}R^n\to M$ with relation matrix $A$ (Prop 11.1).
  • Put $A$ into Smith normal form $\operatorname{diag}(d_1\mid\cdots\mid d_m)$ (Thm 10.3) — i.e. choose good bases of $R^m, R^n$.
  • In those bases $M\cong R^n/\operatorname{im}\psi\cong\bigoplus R/d_iR\oplus R^{n-m}$; drop the unit $d_i$ (where $R/d_iR=0$) and relabel the survivors $c_i$; the leftover free part is $R^s$.
CorCorollaries: free-ness & abelian groups (11.6, 11.7)
  • 11.6: a f.g. torsion-free module over a PID is free; in general the free rank $s=\operatorname{rk}(M/M^{\mathrm{tor}})$ (so $s$ is well-defined). [The torsion part is the $\bigoplus R/c_iR$ summand.]
  • 11.7 (f.g. abelian groups): $A\cong\mathbb Z^r\oplus\mathbb Z/c_1\oplus\cdots\oplus\mathbb Z/c_k$ with $c_1\mid\cdots\mid c_k$ (all $\gt1$), uniquely — just Thm 11.4 with $R=\mathbb Z$, signs fixed by taking $c_i\gt0$.
Lem+ThmPrimary decomposition form (11.8–11.11)
Lemma 11.8 (CRT for cyclic modules): $d_1,\dots,d_k$ pairwise coprime ⇒ $R/(d_1\cdots d_k)\cong\bigoplus R/d_iR$; so $R/cR\cong\bigoplus R/p_i^{r_i}R$ for the prime factorisation of $c$. Thm 11.9: hence $$M\;\cong\;\bigoplus_{i=1}^k R/p_i^{r_i}R\;\oplus\;R^s\qquad(p_i\text{ irreducible, not necessarily distinct}).$$
  • Two forms, one module: invariant factors $c_1\mid\cdots\mid c_r$ = fewest cyclic summands (and algorithmic, via Smith); primary $p_i^{r_i}$ = most summands (but needs factorisation, which isn't automatic). Linked by the CRT.
  • Ex 11.10: $\mathbb Z/44\oplus\mathbb Z/66\cong\mathbb Z/22\oplus\mathbb Z/132$ (invariant, $22\mid132$) $\cong\mathbb Z/2\oplus\mathbb Z/4\oplus\mathbb Z/3\oplus(\mathbb Z/11)^2$ (primary — note $2$ at two powers).

12  Rational & Jordan canonical forms

LemmaLinear maps = torsion $k[t]$-modules (12.1, 12.2)
Via Bridge 2 (§7), a pair $(V,T)$ with $V$ finite-dim is exactly a finitely generated torsion $k[t]$-module ($T$-invariant subspaces = submodules). The companion matrix $C(f)$ of a monic $f=t^n+\sum a_it^i$ is the matrix of "multiply by $t$" on $k[t]/\langle f\rangle$ in the basis $1,t,\dots,t^{n-1}$; for $f=(t-\lambda)^n$ the basis $\{(t-\lambda)^k\}$ (decreasing) gives the Jordan block $J_n(\lambda)$ (Lemma 12.3).
ThmRational canonical form (+ Jordan) (12.4, 12.5)
Hyp: $V$ finite-dimensional over any field $k$, $T:V\to V$ linear.
RCF: there are unique monic $f_1\mid f_2\mid\cdots\mid f_k$ and a basis in which $T$ is block-diagonal with companion blocks $C(f_i)$. Over an algebraically closed field, using the primary form instead gives Jordan normal form (blocks $J_r(\lambda)$).
Sketch — the payoff of the whole course
  • Apply the structure theorem to the torsion $k[t]$-module $(V,T)$: $V\cong\bigoplus k[t]/\langle f_i\rangle$ (invariant factor form), $f_1\mid\cdots\mid f_k$ monic.
  • On each cyclic summand, "multiply by $t$" $=T$ has matrix $C(f_i)$ (Lemma 12.3(1)); assemble the bases ⇒ block-diagonal RCF.
  • Jordan: over $\bar k$ the irreducibles are $t-\lambda$, so the primary form gives summands $k[t]/\langle(t-\lambda)^r\rangle$, on which $t$ acts as $J_r(\lambda)$ (Lemma 12.3(2)).
  • Computation (12.5): the relation matrix of $(V,T)$ is $tI-A$ over $k[t]$; its Smith normal form gives the invariant factors directly. (Ex 12.6: $tI-A$ reduces to $\operatorname{diag}(1,1,\,t^3+3t^2+2t+1)$, so $A$ is already a companion matrix.)
  • RCF works over any field — its advantage over Jordan, which needs the eigenvalues to live in $k$. (See the Linear Algebra notes for the eigenvalue-side derivation; this is the module-theoretic one.)

13–15  Appendices

App APolynomial rings, group rings, convolution (§13) non-exam
  • $R[[t]]$ = all sequences $\mathbb N\to R$ under convolution; $R[t]$ = the finitely-supported subring. Fixes "polynomials aren't functions" (over finite rings) by defining them as coefficient sequences; evaluation then recovers the function on any ring.
  • Group ring $R[G]$ = finitely-supported $f:G\to R$ with convolution $(f*g)(x)=\sum_{y_1y_2=x}f(y_1)g(y_2)$; identity $\delta_e$. $R[\mathbb Z]=$ Laurent polynomials $R[t,t^{-1}]$. ($\mathbb C[G]$ = the group algebra of representation theory.)
App BUnique factorisation for $\mathbb Z$, concretely (§14)
Re-proves for $\mathbb Z$ what §4–6 did abstractly — and this concrete chain is exactly what motivates the ED/PID/UFD definitions: division algorithm (14.5) ⟹ ideals of $\mathbb Z$ principal + Bézout (14.8) ⟹ irreducible ⟺ prime (14.14) ⟹ Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (14.15).
AppThe two cautionary rings (14.17, §15)
  • $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-17}]$ is not a UFD: $18=2\cdot3\cdot3=(1+\sqrt{-17})(1-\sqrt{-17})$, all irreducible, via the multiplicative norm $N(a+b\sqrt{-17})=a^2+17b^2$ — irreducibles that aren't prime.
  • $\mathbb Z[\tfrac12(1+\sqrt{-19})]$ is a PID but not Euclidean (§15 non-exam): a "weak norm" argument gives PID; a direct check on $2,3,\theta$ rules out any Euclidean function. The witness that PID ⊊ ED is genuinely strict.
